## M26: Budget Planning and Tracking

**Format:** Facilitated session (60 min) with hands-on budget exercise
**Roles:** Owner (Tier 4)
**URL:** `/admin/budget`

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, participants will:

1. Set an annual budget by expense category
2. Track actual spending against budgeted amounts monthly
3. Read and interpret variance reports (over/under budget)
4. Export budget data to Excel for accountant review
5. Compare budget performance across sites (Brooklyn Park vs. Crystal)

### Facilitator Notes

This is the module that replaces "shoebox receipts and quarterly surprise from the accountant." Many childcare owners operate with a general sense of whether they are making or losing money, but no precision. The budget tool provides that precision without requiring an accounting degree.

Start by asking: "What were your total expenses last month? Within $500, can you tell me?" Most owners cannot. That is not a failure. It is a visibility problem. This module solves it.

Do not overwhelm with complexity. The budget tool uses plain categories (Payroll, Food, Supplies, Rent, Utilities, Insurance, Training, Other) rather than accounting codes. The owner enters expected monthly amounts, and the system tracks actuals as expenses are logged.

For the hands-on exercise, use real numbers from the center. If the owner is not comfortable sharing real financials in a group setting, prepare a realistic sample budget based on a 40-child center with 8-10 staff.

### Step-by-Step Platform Walkthrough

**Step 1: Open the Budget Dashboard**
Navigate to `/admin/budget`. The dashboard shows:

- Current month at a glance: total budgeted vs. total actual
- Category-level breakdown with progress bars
- Year-to-date cumulative view
- Variance summary (categories over or under budget)

[Screenshot: Budget dashboard showing current month with category breakdown, progress bars filled to different levels, green for under budget, red for over]

**Step 2: Set or Edit the Annual Budget**
Click "Edit Budget" to open the budget builder. For each category, enter the monthly budgeted amount:

| Category | Monthly Budget | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Payroll & Benefits | $XX,XXX | Largest category; 50-60% of total |
| Food & Kitchen | $X,XXX | CACFP reimbursement offsets this |
| Supplies & Materials | $XXX | Classroom supplies, cleaning products |
| Rent / Mortgage | $X,XXX | Fixed cost |
| Utilities | $XXX | Electric, gas, water, internet |
| Insurance | $XXX | Liability, property, workers comp |
| Professional Development | $XXX | Staff training, conferences |
| Marketing | $XXX | Website, ads, printed materials |
| Maintenance & Repairs | $XXX | Building upkeep, equipment |
| Other / Miscellaneous | $XXX | Buffer for unexpected expenses |

The system auto-calculates your annual total and shows a pie chart of expense distribution. If payroll exceeds 60% of total expenses, the system flags it with a note.

[Screenshot: Budget builder form with monthly amount fields for each category, pie chart of distribution, and annual total]

**Step 3: Track Monthly Actuals**
As expenses are logged in the system (through payroll processing, purchase orders, or manual entry), actuals populate automatically. The dashboard updates in real time.

Each category shows:

- Budgeted amount for the month
- Actual spent so far
- Remaining budget
- Percentage consumed (with progress bar)
- Variance: positive (under budget) or negative (over budget)

Click any category to drill into line-item detail. See exactly which expenses contributed to the total.

[Screenshot: Category drill-down for "Food & Kitchen" showing individual expense entries with dates, amounts, and descriptions]

**Step 4: Interpret Variance Reports**
Click "Variance Report" in the top toolbar. The variance view highlights:

- Categories over budget by more than 10% (red)
- Categories under budget by more than 10% (blue)
- Categories within 10% of budget (green)

For each variance, the system shows the dollar amount and percentage. Click any item to see trend data: is this a one-month spike or a 3-month pattern?

The 10% threshold is the investigation trigger. Anything over 10% deserves a "why."

[Screenshot: Variance report with color-coded rows, dollar and percentage variance columns, and trend arrows for 3-month direction]

**Step 5: Compare Sites**
If you operate multiple centers, click "Site Comparison" to view budgets side by side. The comparison shows:

- Per-child cost by category (normalizes for different enrollment sizes)
- Category variance by site
- Total expense per site

This view answers: "Why does Brooklyn Park spend $200 more per month on supplies than Crystal?" Maybe it is a purchasing problem. Maybe it is a classroom count difference. The data tells you where to look.

[Screenshot: Site comparison view with two columns (Brooklyn Park, Crystal) showing per-child cost by category with highlight on largest differences]

**Step 6: Export to Excel**
Click "Export" to download the budget data as an Excel file (.xlsx) or CSV. The export includes:

- Monthly budgeted amounts
- Monthly actual amounts
- Variance calculations
- Year-to-date summaries
- Category breakdowns

Share this with your accountant, bookkeeper, or business advisor. The export matches standard accounting category formats.

[Screenshot: Export dialog with format options (Excel, CSV, PDF) and date range selector]

### Budget Variance Analysis Exercise

**Hands-On Exercise (20 minutes)**

Using the sample data below (or your real data), complete the variance analysis worksheet.

**Sample Monthly Budget vs. Actual:**

| Category | Budget | Actual | Variance ($) | Variance (%) | Flag |
|----------|--------|--------|-------------|-------------|------|
| Payroll | $18,000 | $19,200 | -$1,200 | -6.7% | |
| Food | $2,400 | $2,100 | +$300 | +12.5% | |
| Supplies | $800 | $1,350 | -$550 | -68.8% | RED |
| Rent | $3,500 | $3,500 | $0 | 0% | |
| Utilities | $600 | $580 | +$20 | +3.3% | |
| Insurance | $900 | $900 | $0 | 0% | |
| Training | $400 | $150 | +$250 | +62.5% | BLUE |
| Marketing | $300 | $475 | -$175 | -58.3% | RED |
| Maintenance | $500 | $1,100 | -$600 | -120% | RED |
| Other | $200 | $320 | -$120 | -60% | RED |
| **TOTAL** | **$27,600** | **$29,675** | **-$2,075** | **-7.5%** | |

**Analysis Questions:**

1. Which category has the largest dollar variance? What questions would you ask to understand why?

2. Supplies is 68.8% over budget. Write down 3 possible explanations and the one action you would take this week to investigate.

3. Training is 62.5% under budget. Is that a good thing or a bad thing? What is the long-term risk of consistently underspending on training?

4. Maintenance is $600 over budget this month. Before you panic, what data would you look at to determine if this is a one-time event or a trend?

5. Overall, the center is $2,075 (7.5%) over budget this month. If this trend continues for 12 months, what is the annual impact? Write one sentence describing what you would change.

**Debrief:** Share your analysis with the group. There are no wrong answers on the "why." The skill is asking the right questions and knowing where to look in the system for answers.

### Discussion Questions

1. "What is the single largest budget surprise you have experienced in the past year? With monthly tracking, how many months earlier could you have spotted it?"

2. "Your accountant tells you that you were $15,000 over budget last year. With this system, at what point during the year would you have known that was happening?"

3. "How do you decide when an over-budget category is a problem vs. a reasonable investment? What criteria do you use?"

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Setting the budget once and never revisiting | "I did the budget in January" | Review budget vs. actual on the 1st of every month. The budget is a living document, not a January task. |
| Not investigating variances | "We are kind of close" | Any category over 10% deserves investigation. Click into the line items. Five minutes of investigation prevents months of overspending. |
| Underbudgeting payroll | Forgot about benefits, overtime, or new hires | Budget payroll at 105% of calculated amount to account for overtime, coverage needs, and benefit costs. |
| Ignoring the site comparison | "Each center is different" | Yes, and the comparison shows you exactly how different. A $200/month supply cost difference across 12 months is $2,400. That adds up. |

### Handout: Monthly Budget Review Checklist

**1st of Every Month (30 minutes)**

- [ ] Open `/admin/budget`
- [ ] Review overall budget vs. actual for last month
- [ ] Flag any category over 10% variance
- [ ] Drill into flagged categories; review line items
- [ ] Compare sites if applicable
- [ ] Note 1-2 action items (investigate, adjust, or approve)
- [ ] Export data for accountant if needed

**Quarterly (60 minutes)**

- [ ] Review year-to-date cumulative budget
- [ ] Identify trending variances (same category over budget 3+ months)
- [ ] Adjust budget amounts if circumstances have changed (new hire, rate increase, enrollment shift)
- [ ] Meet with accountant to reconcile system data with books

## M27: Revenue Forecasting

**Format:** Facilitated session (45 min)
**Roles:** Owner (Tier 4)
**URL:** `/admin/financial/forecasting`

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, participants will:

1. Read the revenue projection dashboard
2. Understand what drives revenue changes (enrollment, rates, CACFP, authorizations)
3. Run "what-if" scenario models to test decisions before making them
4. Interpret the profit-and-loss (P&L) summary
5. Use forecasting to plan 3, 6, and 12 months ahead

### Facilitator Notes

This module teaches the owner to think forward instead of backward. Budget tracking (M26) tells you where money went. Forecasting tells you where money is going. Together, they give you control.

The "what-if" scenario tool is the centerpiece of this module. It lets the owner model decisions before committing to them. "What happens if I lose 3 children next month?" "What happens if I raise rates by $25/week?" "What if I hire a part-time floater?" These are real questions with real financial consequences. The system calculates the impact in seconds.

Start the facilitated session with a scenario, not a lecture: "Enrollment drops by 3 children next month. How does that affect your ability to make payroll in 60 days?" Walk through the math together, then show how the system does that math automatically.

### Step-by-Step Platform Walkthrough

**Step 1: Open the Forecasting Dashboard**
Navigate to `/admin/financial/forecasting`. The dashboard shows:

- Monthly revenue projection (next 12 months)
- Revenue by source (tuition, CACFP, authorizations, other)
- Enrollment trend line (actual enrollment with projected trajectory)
- Monthly P&L summary (revenue minus expenses)

[Screenshot: Forecasting dashboard with 12-month revenue bar chart, revenue source pie chart, and enrollment trend line]

**Step 2: Understand Revenue Drivers**
The system breaks revenue into components:

| Revenue Source | What Drives It | Where It Comes From |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Tuition (Private Pay) | Enrollment count x weekly rate | Family payments |
| CACFP Reimbursement | Meal counts x reimbursement rate | Federal program (M06, M14) |
| Authorized Care | Authorization count x authorized rate | County/state programs (M24) |
| Registration Fees | New enrollments x fee amount | One-time at enrollment |
| Late Pickup Fees | Late events x fee amount | Incidental |

Click any source in the breakdown to see the underlying data. Tuition revenue, for example, shows each enrolled family, their rate, and their projected annual contribution.

[Screenshot: Revenue source drill-down for tuition showing family list with weekly rate, annual projection, and enrollment status]

**Step 3: Run "What-If" Scenarios**
Click "Scenarios" in the top toolbar. The scenario builder lets you model changes and see their financial impact before making any real decisions.

**Scenario 1: Enrollment Change**
- Adjust the enrollment slider: what happens if you gain or lose 1-5 children?
- The system recalculates monthly revenue, identifies the break-even enrollment number, and shows how many months until the change is fully felt.

**Scenario 2: Rate Adjustment**
- Enter a proposed rate increase (dollar amount or percentage)
- The system shows: new monthly revenue, annual revenue impact, and competitive comparison (if market data is available)
- It also estimates enrollment risk: "Rate increases above $X/week historically correlate with Y% enrollment attrition in similar markets"

**Scenario 3: Staffing Change**
- Add or remove a staff position
- The system recalculates labor cost, checks ratio compliance, and shows the net impact on monthly P&L
- A part-time floater at $16/hour for 20 hours/week costs $16,640/year but saves $X in overtime avoidance

**Scenario 4: Program Change**
- Model adding or removing a classroom/age group
- The system calculates space utilization, required staffing, potential enrollment, and net revenue

Each scenario shows a side-by-side comparison: "Current state" vs. "With this change."

[Screenshot: Scenario builder with enrollment slider adjusted to -3 children, showing current vs. projected monthly revenue and break-even analysis]

**Step 4: Read the P&L Summary**
The profit-and-loss view consolidates revenue (from forecasting) and expenses (from M26 budget) into one view:

- Total Revenue
- Total Expenses
- Net Income (Revenue minus Expenses)
- Net Margin (Net Income as a percentage of Revenue)
- Monthly trend (are margins improving or declining?)

A healthy childcare center operates at 10-15% net margin. Below 5% means one bad month could create a cash crisis. Above 15% means you may have room to invest in quality improvements.

[Screenshot: P&L summary showing 12-month trend of revenue, expenses, and net income with margin percentage]

**Step 5: Plan Ahead**
Use the 3/6/12 month view to project forward:

- **3 months:** Immediate decisions (hire/fire, rate changes, enrollment targets)
- **6 months:** Seasonal planning (summer enrollment dip, back-to-school surge, holiday staffing)
- **12 months:** Strategic planning (expansion, program changes, capital investments)

The system highlights known seasonal patterns based on your historical data. If enrollment historically drops 10% in June, the forecast reflects that without you having to remember.

[Screenshot: 12-month forecast view with seasonal pattern overlays and key decision points marked]

### Discussion Questions

1. "If enrollment drops by 3 children next month, how many months of savings do you have before payroll becomes a problem? Run the scenario and find out."

2. "You are considering a $25/week rate increase. What is the break-even point: how many families would need to leave before the increase costs you money? The scenario tool answers this in 10 seconds."

3. "What is your net margin right now? Is it where you want it to be? What is one lever you could pull to improve it by 2 percentage points?"

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Never running scenarios | "I'll figure it out when it happens" | Run at least one scenario per month. The 5 minutes it takes prevents decisions based on gut feeling alone. |
| Ignoring CACFP revenue in projections | "It's a small amount" | CACFP can represent $40,000-80,000/year. Undercounting it skews your entire forecast. |
| Making rate decisions without modeling | "Everyone else raised their rates" | Model the rate increase first. See the impact. Then decide. Your center's enrollment sensitivity may be different from the center down the street. |
| Not accounting for seasonal patterns | "July will be the same as March" | It will not. Summer enrollment dips are predictable. Plan staffing reductions or camp programs to match. |

### Handout: Revenue Forecasting Monthly Routine

**Monthly (20 minutes)**

- [ ] Open `/admin/financial/forecasting`
- [ ] Review revenue projection vs. last month's actual
- [ ] Check enrollment trend: gaining, losing, or stable?
- [ ] Run one "what-if" scenario relevant to a current decision
- [ ] Review P&L: is margin above 10%?
- [ ] Note any action items

**Before Any Major Decision**

Run the scenario first. This applies to:

- Hiring or letting go of staff
- Changing tuition rates
- Adding or closing a classroom
- Making a capital purchase over $1,000
- Signing a new lease or contract

**Seasonal Planning Calendar**

| Month | Revenue Pattern | Action |
|-------|----------------|--------|
| January | Post-holiday dip | Review annual budget |
| March | Spring enrollment inquiries | Push tour scheduling |
| May | Summer transition planning | Model summer enrollment |
| August | Back-to-school surge | Staff up for September |
| November | Holiday schedule impact | Budget for year-end bonuses |

2. Revenue source drill-down
3. Scenario builder with enrollment change modeled
4. Scenario builder with rate adjustment modeled

## M28: Cost Optimization

**Format:** Facilitated session (45 min)
**Roles:** Owner (Tier 4)
**URL:** `/admin/financial` (expense breakdown view)

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, participants will:

1. Read the expense breakdown and identify the largest cost drivers
2. Calculate labor cost as a percentage of revenue
3. Identify 3 specific optimization opportunities from their own data
4. Understand the difference between cost cutting (harmful) and cost optimization (strategic)
5. Use platform data to find savings that are invisible in manual bookkeeping

### Facilitator Notes

Cost optimization is not cost cutting. Cost cutting reduces quality. Cost optimization finds waste and redirects resources to where they matter. This distinction is critical. The goal is never to spend less on children. The goal is to stop spending money on things that do not serve children or families.

The platform reveals patterns that paper bookkeeping hides: overtime that accumulates slowly, food waste from inaccurate meal counts, supply spending that varies 3x between classrooms doing the same activities, and training budgets that go unspent while staff certifications expire.

This module works best with real data from the center. If the owner is willing, pull up the actual expense breakdown during the session. The "aha" moments come from seeing their own numbers, not hypothetical examples.

### Step-by-Step Platform Walkthrough

**Step 1: Open the Expense Breakdown**
Navigate to `/admin/financial` and click the expense breakdown view. The system displays:

- Total expenses by category (bar chart)
- Percentage of total for each category
- Month-over-month trend for each category
- Per-child cost calculation (total expenses / enrolled children)

[Screenshot: Expense breakdown bar chart with category percentages, per-child cost callout, and month-over-month trend arrows]

**Step 2: Analyze Labor Costs**
Labor is the single largest expense (typically 50-60% of revenue). The labor breakdown shows:

- Regular hours cost
- Overtime cost (highlighted separately)
- Benefits cost
- Per-classroom labor cost
- Labor cost as % of revenue

The target: labor at 50-56% of revenue. Above 60% means either staffing is inefficient or revenue is too low. The system flags which problem it is.

Overtime deserves special attention. Consistent overtime (same staff, every week) signals a scheduling problem, not a workload problem. Inconsistent overtime (spikes during certain weeks) may signal sick-call coverage issues.

[Screenshot: Labor cost breakdown showing regular vs. overtime split, per-classroom costs, and labor-as-percentage-of-revenue gauge]

**Step 3: CACFP Maximization Check**
Click "CACFP Revenue" in the expense view. The system shows:

- Maximum possible CACFP reimbursement (based on enrollment and meal eligibility)
- Actual CACFP revenue received
- Capture rate (actual / maximum)
- Revenue left on the table (maximum minus actual)

A capture rate below 90% means money is being lost to missed meal counts (M06), incomplete documentation, or children eligible but not enrolled in the program. Each percentage point of capture rate improvement is $400-800/year for a 40-child center.

[Screenshot: CACFP capture rate gauge showing actual vs. maximum with dollar amount of revenue left on the table]

**Step 4: Supply Efficiency Analysis**
The supply breakdown shows spending by classroom and by category (art supplies, cleaning products, diapers, food items, office supplies).

Look for:

- Classrooms spending 2x+ more than similar classrooms on the same items
- Supply categories with consistent month-over-month increases
- Bulk purchasing opportunities (items bought frequently in small quantities)

The system highlights anomalies: "Toddler Room spent $180 on art supplies this month vs. $65 average for toddler rooms."

[Screenshot: Supply spending comparison across classrooms with anomaly highlight on one classroom]

**Step 5: Identify Optimization Opportunities**
The system generates an "Optimization Suggestions" panel based on your data. Examples:

- "Overtime cost was $1,200 last month. Hiring a part-time floater at $800/month would reduce overtime and improve coverage."
- "CACFP capture rate is 82%. Improving to 90% would add $3,200/year in reimbursement."
- "Supply spending in Preschool Room A is 2.4x the average. Investigate purchasing patterns."
- "3 staff certifications are within 90 days of expiry. Budget $600 for renewal training."

These are not commands. They are conversation starters. The owner decides which suggestions to act on.

[Screenshot: Optimization suggestions panel with 4 data-driven recommendations, each showing dollar impact]

**Step 6: Build an Action Plan**
For each optimization opportunity you choose to pursue:

1. Note the current state (what the data shows)
2. Define the target (what "better" looks like in numbers)
3. Identify the action (what changes)
4. Set a timeline (when you will measure again)
5. Assign ownership (who is responsible)

Track these actions as tasks in the platform (M08) so they appear on the task board and have accountability.

### Discussion Questions

1. "Look at your labor cost as a percentage of revenue. Is it where you want it to be? If it is 60%, what would need to change to bring it to 55% without reducing quality?"

2. "What is one expense category where you suspect waste but have never had the data to prove it? Let's look at the data right now."

3. "The platform shows that your CACFP capture rate is 85%. That means you are leaving approximately $X,XXX on the table annually. What is the root cause, and what would it take to get to 95%?"

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Cutting training budget to save money | It is the easiest line item to defer | Cutting training leads to expired certifications, which leads to staffing crises. Training is not an expense; it is insurance. |
| Ignoring small savings | "$50/month is not worth worrying about" | $50/month x 12 months x 3 categories = $1,800/year. Small savings compound. |
| Reducing staff to save on labor | Panic response to high labor costs | Reducing below required ratios is illegal. Optimizing schedules (M18) to reduce overtime is legal and effective. |
| Looking at cost optimization once a year | "We do a budget review in January" | Cost patterns shift monthly. Quarterly review catches problems before they become annual surprises. |

**Format:** Guided analysis with facilitator
