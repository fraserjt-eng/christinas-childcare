## M29: Cross-Site Operations

**Format:** Self-paced (15 min) + facilitated strategy session (45 min)
**Roles:** Owner (Tier 4)
**URL:** `/admin/operations`

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, participants will:

1. Monitor both centers from a single dashboard view
2. Compare key performance metrics between Brooklyn Park and Crystal
3. Apply the management-by-exception framework to daily operations
4. Identify when to intervene vs. when to trust the director
5. Reduce driving between locations by using the dual-center view for remote oversight

### Facilitator Notes

This module is about the owner's relationship with time. An owner who drives between two locations to "check on things" spends 10+ hours per week in transit and supervision that the platform can handle from a phone screen. The cross-site dashboard is not about micromanagement; it is about knowing when something needs your attention and when it does not.

The management-by-exception framework is the core concept here. Most metrics will be green most days. The skill is training your eye to notice the yellow and red, taking action on those, and leaving the green alone.

Start the facilitated session with this question: "What made you drive to the other center this week? Was it planned, or was it reactive?" Track the answers. Most unplanned trips happen because the owner did not have visibility into a problem until it escalated.

**Step 5: Set Up Custom Alerts**
Click "Alert Settings" to configure which events trigger notifications on your phone:

**Always notify (non-negotiable):**
- Ratio violation at either center
- Incident report filed (any severity)
- CACFP deadline missed

**Recommended notifications:**
- Task overdue by more than 2 hours
- Staff no-show (scheduled but not clocked in by 15 min past start)
- Parent message unread for more than 4 hours
- Budget category exceeds monthly threshold

**Optional (can be noisy):**
- Every attendance check-in/out
- Every meal count submission
- Every task completed

Start with the "always" and "recommended" lists. Adjust after one week based on volume.

### The Management-by-Exception Framework

This framework changes how you spend your leadership time.

**Traditional Management:** Review everything, check on everyone, be present at both locations, attend to all details personally.

**Management by Exception:** Trust systems and people for normal operations. Reserve your attention for exceptions: things that are off-track, unexpected, or require your authority.

**In Practice:**

| Situation | Traditional Response | Exception-Based Response |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Both centers report green status | Drive to each location to verify | Check dashboard from phone. Spend the morning on strategic work. |
| Crystal has a yellow ratio alert | Call the director to ask what is happening | Click the alert. See that a staff member is 10 minutes late. Monitor for 15 minutes. If it turns red, then call. |
| Brooklyn Park has a red incident report | Drop everything and drive over | Read the full report in the system. If severity is critical, go. If moderate, message the director for details and monitor. |
| Monthly budget is 8% over at one site | Wait for the accountant to notice next quarter | See the variance on the dashboard. Schedule a 15-minute call with the director to understand why. |

**The Three Questions of Exception Management:**

1. Is this a red-level exception that requires me personally? (Go.)
2. Is this a yellow-level exception that the director can handle? (Message and monitor.)
3. Is this a green-level metric that does not need my attention? (Trust the system.)

### Discussion Questions

1. "How many hours per week do you currently spend physically present at each location? What would you do with 5 of those hours back?"

2. "Think about the last time you intervened at a center. Was it a genuine emergency, or could the director have handled it if they had the right tools and authority?"

3. "What would need to be true for you to trust the dashboard enough to start your morning from home one day per week instead of driving to a site?"

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Checking the dashboard every 15 minutes | Anxiety about being out of the loop | Set specific check-in times: 8 AM, noon, 4 PM. Three checks per day is enough unless you receive an alert. |
| Overriding the director when a metric turns yellow | Habit of hands-on control | Yellow is the director's domain. Give them the chance to resolve it. Intervene only when yellow turns red or stays yellow for more than 4 hours. |
| Ignoring the dashboard because "I know my centers" | Resistance to data | You know your centers today. The dashboard knows them in real time. It catches things you miss when you are at the other location. |
| Using the dashboard for surveillance instead of support | Trust deficit with directors | The dashboard is a tool for you, not a tool against them. Share the same view with directors so they see what you see. Transparency builds trust. |

### Handout: Daily Cross-Site Routine

**Morning Check (8:00 AM, 5 minutes, from anywhere)**

- [ ] Open `/admin/operations` on your phone
- [ ] Scan both center panels for red indicators
- [ ] Address any red items immediately (call, message, or drive)
- [ ] Note any yellow items; set a mental check-back for noon
- [ ] Confirm attendance and staffing look normal at both sites

**Midday Check (12:00 PM, 5 minutes)**

- [ ] Re-check any yellow items from morning
- [ ] Confirm meal counts were submitted on time at both centers
- [ ] Review any new incidents or messages
- [ ] If all green, move on. If yellow persists, message the director.

**Afternoon Check (4:00 PM, 5 minutes)**

- [ ] Review task completion for the day
- [ ] Check for any late afternoon issues (staff leaving early, pickup delays)
- [ ] Note anything to address tomorrow

**Weekly (Monday, 30 minutes)**

- [ ] Review the site comparison metrics
- [ ] Identify the largest gap between centers
- [ ] Schedule a 15-minute call with each director to discuss their center's data
- [ ] Set one improvement goal per center for the week

## M30: Strategic Planning and Continuous Improvement

**Format:** Facilitated PD session (90 min)
**Roles:** Owner (Tier 4)
**URL:** `/admin/strategic` (strategic plan), `/admin/meetings` (meeting efficiency tool)

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, participants will:

1. Define or refine mission, vision, and values in the system
2. Conduct a SWOT analysis using platform data
3. Set strategic priorities with deadlines and accountability
4. Run efficient leadership meetings using the meeting tool
5. Create action items that automatically feed the task board
6. Build a quarterly review rhythm for continuous improvement

### Facilitator Notes

This is the capstone module for the entire curriculum. Everything the owner has learned in Units 1-7 feeds into this module. Budget data informs strategic priorities. Pipeline data reveals growth opportunities. Staff metrics surface operational improvements. The strategic plan ties it all together.

The 90-minute session should produce a real strategic plan, not a practice one. By this point, the owner has enough platform familiarity to populate the plan with actual data. The plan does not need to be perfect. It needs to exist, be written down, and have dates attached to each priority.

Start with this framing: "A strategic plan is not a document you write once and file away. It is a living list of priorities with names and dates attached. Every time you update it, your business gets more intentional."

### Step-by-Step Platform Walkthrough

**Step 1: Access the Strategic Planning Page**
Navigate to `/admin/strategic`. The strategic planning page has four sections:

1. **Foundation:** Mission, Vision, Values
2. **Analysis:** SWOT assessment
3. **Priorities:** Strategic goals with timelines
4. **Action Items:** Tasks that flow to the task board

[Screenshot: Strategic planning page with four-section layout, foundation at top, SWOT in the middle, priorities and actions at the bottom]

**Step 2: Define Mission, Vision, and Values**
The Foundation section contains editable fields for:

- **Mission:** What you do and who you serve (1-2 sentences)
- **Vision:** What success looks like in 3-5 years (1-2 sentences)
- **Values:** The 3-5 principles that guide decisions

These fields may already be populated from your initial platform setup. Review them with fresh eyes. After working through the entire curriculum, your understanding of the business is sharper. Do these statements still reflect reality?

Enter or edit each field. The system saves automatically. These statements appear on your admin dashboard and can be shared with staff.

[Screenshot: Foundation section with mission, vision, and values fields, one partially edited with cursor visible]

**Step 3: Conduct a SWOT Analysis**
Click "SWOT Analysis" to open the four-quadrant assessment tool:

| Internal | |
|----------|--|
| **Strengths** (What we do well) | **Weaknesses** (Where we struggle) |
| | |

| External | |
|----------|--|
| **Opportunities** (What we could do) | **Threats** (What could hurt us) |
| | |

For each quadrant, add items by clicking "+" and typing. The system suggests items based on your platform data:

**Data-Driven SWOT Suggestions:**

- *Strength:* "CACFP capture rate is 94% (above 90% benchmark)"
- *Weakness:* "Average tour-to-enroll conversion is 32% (below 50% benchmark)"
- *Opportunity:* "3 families on waitlist for Toddler Room; could open a second section"
- *Threat:* "2 staff certifications expiring in 45 days with no renewal scheduled"

You can accept, modify, or dismiss each suggestion. Add your own items that the data does not capture: community relationships, reputation, competitive landscape, personal goals.

### Step-by-Step Platform Walkthrough (continued)

**Step 4: Set Strategic Priorities**
Click "Add Priority" to create strategic goals. Each priority includes:

- **Goal statement:** What you want to achieve (specific and measurable)
- **Category:** Growth, Operations, Financial, Quality, Compliance
- **Timeline:** Target completion date
- **Owner:** Who is responsible (you, a director, or shared)
- **Key metrics:** How you will know it is working (pulled from platform data)
- **Status:** Not Started, In Progress, On Track, At Risk, Complete

Example priorities:

| Priority | Category | Timeline | Owner | Key Metric |
|----------|----------|----------|-------|-----------|
| Increase enrollment to 45 children | Growth | 6 months | Owner + Directors | Pipeline conversion rate |
| Reduce overtime to $500/month | Financial | 3 months | Owner | Monthly overtime cost |
| Achieve 95% CACFP capture rate | Compliance | 3 months | Directors | CACFP dashboard |
| Hire and onboard 2 new staff | Operations | 2 months | Owner | Onboarding completion |
| Launch weekly parent newsletter | Quality | 1 month | Director (BP) | Newsletter send rate |

Each priority appears on your dashboard with progress tracking.

[Screenshot: Strategic priorities list with 5 goals, timeline bars, owner names, and status indicators]

**Step 5: Use the Meeting Efficiency Tool**
Navigate to `/admin/meetings`. The meeting tool helps you run tighter leadership meetings by:

**Before the meeting:**
- Create an agenda with time blocks for each topic
- Attach relevant data (pull metrics, reports, or dashboard snapshots)
- Assign agenda items to presenters
- Set a meeting duration target

**During the meeting:**
- Timer tracks each agenda item
- Participants can add notes directly in the system
- Action items are created in real time (not written on a whiteboard and forgotten)
- Parking lot captures off-topic items for later

**After the meeting:**
- Summary auto-generates with decisions, action items, and owners
- Action items push to the task board (M08) with deadlines
- Follow-up reminders auto-send before the next meeting

[Screenshot: Meeting tool showing agenda with time blocks, timer running, and action item sidebar]

**Step 6: Action-Item-to-Task Workflow**
This is where strategic planning connects to daily operations. Every action item created during a meeting or from a strategic priority automatically appears on the task board.

The workflow:

1. During a meeting, you note: "Sarah will research floater candidates by April 15"
2. The system creates a task: assigned to Sarah, due April 15, category "Strategic"
3. Sarah sees it on her task board at `/employee/tasks`
4. You see completion status on your dashboard
5. At the next meeting, the system shows which action items from last meeting are complete, in progress, or overdue

This closes the loop between "we talked about it" and "it actually happened."

[Screenshot: Action item from meeting appearing as a task on the task board with meeting reference link]

**Step 7: Build a Quarterly Review Rhythm**
Set recurring calendar entries (or use the meeting tool) for:

**Monthly Strategic Check-In (30 minutes)**
- Review priority status (on track, at risk?)
- Check key metrics for each priority
- Adjust timelines if circumstances changed
- Add new priorities if something urgent emerged

**Quarterly SWOT Refresh (60 minutes)**
- Revisit each quadrant
- Remove items that are no longer relevant
- Add new items based on the last 3 months of data
- Re-prioritize the top item in each quadrant

**Annual Vision Review (90 minutes)**
- Do the mission and vision still fit?
- Review the full year's data: enrollment trends, financial performance, staff retention
- Set the next year's strategic priorities
- Celebrate what worked; learn from what did not

### Discussion Questions

1. "What is the one strategic priority that, if you accomplished it in the next 90 days, would have the biggest impact on your business? Write it down right now."

2. "How many leadership meetings in the past year produced action items that were actually completed? What is different about the ones that were?"

3. "If you had to explain your 3-year vision for these two centers in two sentences, what would you say? Does your daily work align with that vision, or are you spending most of your time reacting?"

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Setting too many priorities | Ambition | Three to five priorities per quarter. If everything is a priority, nothing is. |
| Writing vague goals | Not sure how to make it specific | Every goal needs a number and a date. "Improve enrollment" becomes "Enroll 5 new families by June 30." |
| Conducting SWOT without data | Going off gut feeling | Pull platform data first. Strengths and weaknesses should be backed by numbers. Gut feeling confirms data; it does not replace it. |
| Skipping the quarterly review | "Things are going fine" | Strategic drift is invisible until it is not. The quarterly review takes 60 minutes and prevents months of misalignment. |
| Creating action items without owners | "We all know what needs to happen" | No owner means nobody is accountable. Every action item gets a name and a date. Period. |
| Having meetings without the meeting tool | "We'll just talk" | Unstructured meetings run long, produce vague commitments, and leave no record. The meeting tool is a 2-minute setup that saves 20 minutes of wasted meeting time. |

**Meeting Efficiency Rules**

1. Every meeting has a written agenda shared before the meeting
2. Every agenda item has a time block (5, 10, or 15 minutes)
3. Every discussion produces either a decision or an action item
4. Every action item has a name and a date
5. Every meeting ends with a summary of decisions and next steps
6. Every next meeting starts with reviewing last meeting's action items

## Unit 8 Capstone Exercise

**Time:** 45 minutes
**Format:** Facilitated strategic planning session

This is the capstone for the entire Owner Pathway. Complete these tasks using the platform:

1. **Cross-Site Check (M29):** Open the dual-center dashboard. Identify one exception at each center. Describe your response for each using the management-by-exception framework.

2. **SWOT Analysis (M30):** Complete all four quadrants using platform data. Identify the top priority from your SWOT.

3. **Strategic Priority (M30):** Write one strategic priority with a measurable goal, deadline, owner, and key metric.

4. **Meeting Setup (M30):** Create an agenda for a 30-minute leadership meeting to discuss your top priority. Include 3 agenda items with time blocks.

5. **Action Item (M30):** Create one action item from your meeting agenda that feeds the task board.

**Success Criteria:**

- Dashboard exceptions identified with appropriate-level responses
- SWOT completed with at least 3 items per quadrant, including data-backed items
- One strategic priority written with all required fields
- Meeting agenda created with time blocks and assigned items
- Action item visible on the task board with owner and deadline
