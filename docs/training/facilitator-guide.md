# Facilitator Guide: Christina's Child Care Center Platform Training

This guide is for whoever runs the live training sessions. That might be the owner, a site director, or an outside consultant. You do not need formal training experience to use this guide. Every session is scripted with timing, talking points, and activities. Follow the plan, stay on schedule, and trust the structure.

**How to use this guide:** Find the module you are facilitating. Read the full session plan at least once before the session. Gather materials. Print any handouts. Set up the room. Then walk through the timed agenda step by step.

**General facilitation tips:**
- Start on time, every time. Respect people's schedules.
- Say "Let me show you" instead of "You should know this." Nobody wants to feel behind.
- When someone asks a question you do not know the answer to, say so. Write it on the parking lot and follow up within 48 hours.
- Keep a "parking lot" (whiteboard corner or sticky note) for off-topic questions.
- After every session, take 5 minutes alone to jot down what worked and what to change next time.

## M04: Kiosk Demo

**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Audience:** Parents (Tier 1), Employees (Tier 2), Directors (Tier 3)
**Best delivered:** During orientation or first day at the center, one-on-one or small group at the kiosk itself

### Materials Needed
- Kiosk device (tablet or mounted screen), powered on and logged in
- Printed PIN card for each participant (pre-generated from the system)
- One backup device in case of technical issues

### Room Setup
Standing at the kiosk. No chairs needed. Keep the group to 3-4 people max so everyone can see the screen.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:30 | Welcome and context |
| 0:30-2:00 | Live demo: facilitator checks in a child |
| 2:00-4:00 | Each participant practices check-in and check-out |
| 4:00-5:00 | Review auto-reset timer and troubleshooting |

### Facilitator Script

**0:00 Welcome:**
"This is the kiosk you'll use every time you arrive and every time you leave. It replaces the paper sign-in sheet. The reason we switched: paper sheets get lost, handwriting gets unreadable, and when licensing shows up, we need accurate records down to the minute. This gives us that."

**0:30 Live demo:**
"Watch me first. I tap the screen to wake it up. I enter my PIN. I see my children listed. I tap the check-in button next to each child's name. Green checkmark means they are checked in. That is it. When I leave, same process, but I tap check-out."

**2:00 Practice round:**
"Now you try. Use the PIN on your card. Check in, then check out. I will stand right here if you need help."

**4:00 Auto-reset and troubleshooting:**
"If you walk away without finishing, the screen resets after 30 seconds. That protects your information. If you enter the wrong PIN three times, it locks for one minute. If your PIN does not work at all, come find a staff member and we will reset it."

### Discussion Questions
- "What happens if you forget your PIN?" (Expected: Ask a staff member for a reset.)
- "What if you are dropping off but someone else is picking up?" (Expected: The pickup person needs their own PIN. Authorized pickups are set up in the system.)

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"Can I use my phone instead?"** Not for check-in. The kiosk is the official record. You can view attendance history on your phone through the parent portal.
- **"What if the kiosk is broken?"** Staff will record your arrival manually and enter it into the system. There is always a backup process.

### Success Indicators
- Every participant completes a check-in and check-out without help
- Nobody leaves confused about their PIN or the process

## M05: Attendance Scenarios

**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Audience:** Employees (Tier 2), Directors (Tier 3), Owner (Tier 4)

### Materials Needed
- Projector or large screen connected to the platform (attendance dashboard visible)
- One device per participant (phone, tablet, or laptop) logged into the system
- Printed scenario cards (5 scenarios, described below)
- Whiteboard and markers

### Room Setup
Tables arranged in a U-shape so everyone can see the projected screen and each other. Each person has a device in front of them.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:03 | Welcome and session goal |
| 0:03-0:08 | Live dashboard walkthrough |
| 0:08-0:10 | Distribute scenario cards |
| 0:10-0:22 | Scenario rotations (3 min each, 4 scenarios) |
| 0:22-0:27 | Group debrief |
| 0:27-0:30 | Habit commitment and close |

### Facilitator Script

**0:00 Welcome:**
"Today we are going to practice handling real attendance situations using the platform. By the end of this session, you should be able to look at the attendance dashboard and know exactly what is happening in every classroom, and know what to do when something unexpected comes up."

**0:03 Dashboard walkthrough:**
Pull up the attendance dashboard on the projector. Walk through:
- Real-time headcount by classroom
- Color coding (green = in ratio, yellow = close, red = out of ratio)
- Absent list and late arrival indicators
- How to filter by classroom

"This is your morning command center. At 9:30 every day, this screen should be your first stop."

**0:08 Distribute scenario cards:**
"I am going to hand out scenario cards. Each one describes a real situation that happens in childcare. When I call your scenario number, read it out loud, then walk us through what you would do in the system."

### Scenario Cards

**Scenario 1:** A parent calls at 10:15 AM. Their child, Jaylen, will not be coming today because he is sick. You see Jaylen is currently marked as "expected" on the dashboard. What do you do?

*Expected response:* Mark Jaylen as absent-sick in the system. Check if this changes the classroom ratio. Notify the lead teacher if the absence affects staffing.

**Scenario 2:** It is 9:45 AM. You notice that two children in the Toddler Room are showing as "expected" but have not been checked in. Their parents have not called. What do you do?

*Expected response:* Call both families to confirm status. If no answer, try emergency contacts. Update attendance accordingly. Document the outreach.

**Scenario 3:** A new child is starting today. The parent checked in at the kiosk, but the child does not appear on the classroom dashboard. What do you do?

*Expected response:* Check if the child was assigned to the correct classroom in the system. If not, update the classroom assignment. Verify the enrollment record is complete.

**Scenario 4:** Three staff members called in sick. You need to figure out which classrooms can absorb children and which need substitute coverage. How do you use the dashboard?

*Expected response:* Check current ratios per classroom. Identify rooms with ratio headroom. Move children or combine groups where licensing allows. Request subs for rooms that cannot absorb.

### Debrief Protocol

**0:22 Group debrief:**
Ask the group:
- "Which scenario felt hardest? Why?"
- "What information did you need that was hard to find on the dashboard?"
- "What would you do differently in your morning routine based on what we practiced?"

Listen for: confusion about where to find specific data, workarounds people have been using, gaps between the system and their current habits.

**0:27 Habit commitment:**
"Before we wrap up, I want everyone to name one thing they will do differently starting tomorrow. Write it on a sticky note and put it on the board."

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"What if the internet goes down and we cannot access the dashboard?"** Use the printed backup roster (always have one current copy in each classroom). Enter data into the system as soon as connectivity returns.
- **"Do I need to update attendance for every transition?"** No. Check-in and check-out happen at the kiosk. Classroom transitions (like moving to the gym) are tracked by staff updating the room assignment.

### Success Indicators
- Every participant can locate a child's status on the dashboard within 10 seconds
- Participants correctly identify ratio implications in Scenario 4
- At least half the group names a specific habit change during the closing

## M06: CACFP Basics

**Duration:** 45 minutes
**Audience:** Employees (Tier 2), Directors (Tier 3), Owner (Tier 4)

### Materials Needed
- Projector or large screen showing the meal count submission screen
- One device per participant logged into the system
- Printed CACFP deadline reference card (one per person)
- Whiteboard with the following written before the session starts:
  - Breakfast deadline: 9:30 AM
  - Lunch deadline: 1:30 PM
  - Snack deadline: posted per your schedule
- Calculator (for the cost impact exercise)

### Room Setup
Tables in a U-shape. Everyone facing the projected screen with their own device.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:05 | Welcome and "why CACFP matters" context |
| 0:05-0:12 | Live demo: submitting a meal count |
| 0:12-0:17 | Individual practice: submit a sample count |
| 0:17-0:25 | The money math: cost of missed counts |
| 0:25-0:35 | Pair activity: deadline scenarios |
| 0:35-0:42 | Group discussion: barriers and solutions |
| 0:42-0:45 | Habit commitment and close |

### Facilitator Script

**0:00 Welcome and context:**
"CACFP stands for Child and Adult Care Food Program. It is the federal program that reimburses us for meals we serve. Here is the thing most people do not realize: we only get reimbursed for meals we document. If a child eats lunch and nobody submits the count, that meal does not exist as far as the government is concerned. And that costs us real money."

Write on the whiteboard: **$3-8 per child per missed meal**

"A center with 40 kids that misses one meal count per week loses between $6,000 and $16,000 a year. That is a staff position. That is playground equipment. That is money we earned and gave away because of paperwork."

**0:05 Live demo:**
Walk through the meal count submission screen step by step:
1. Select the meal type (breakfast, lunch, AM snack, PM snack)
2. Select the classroom
3. Review the pre-filled count (pulled from attendance)
4. Adjust for children who did not eat (sick, refused, absent)
5. Submit before the deadline
6. Show the confirmation screen and the green checkmark

"Notice the pre-fill. The system already knows who is here today based on check-in data. You are not counting from scratch. You are confirming and adjusting."

**0:12 Individual practice:**
"Open your device. I have loaded a practice day with sample attendance. Submit a breakfast count for your classroom. Raise your hand when you see the green checkmark."

Walk the room. Help anyone who gets stuck. Common sticking points: selecting the wrong meal type, not scrolling down to see all children, forgetting to hit submit.

**0:17 The money math:**
Hand out calculators or let people use their phones.

"Let's do the math for our center. We have [X] children enrolled. If we miss one meal count per classroom per week, and we have [Y] classrooms, here is what that costs us annually."

Write the formula on the whiteboard:
**[missed meals per week] x [avg reimbursement per meal] x 52 weeks = annual loss**

Let participants calculate. Share results. The number is always larger than people expect.

**0:25 Pair activity: deadline scenarios:**
Put participants in pairs. Each pair gets two scenarios:

*Scenario A:* It is 1:25 PM. Lunch is over. You are cleaning up and a child has a bathroom accident. Another child is crying. You have 5 minutes until the deadline. What do you do?

*Expected:* Submit the count first (it takes 60 seconds), then handle the other situations. The count cannot wait; the accident and the crying can be managed by a partner.

*Scenario B:* You submitted the breakfast count but realize you counted 12 children when only 11 actually ate (one child arrived after breakfast). What do you do?

*Expected:* Edit the count before the deadline if possible. If the deadline has passed, notify the director immediately for a correction.

**0:35 Group discussion:**
"What gets in the way of submitting counts on time? Let's be honest."

Listen for: busy meal transitions, forgetting, not having the device nearby, confusion about who is responsible. Write barriers on one side of the whiteboard and solutions on the other.

Common barriers and solutions:
- "I forget" -> Set a recurring alarm on your phone for 5 minutes before each deadline
- "I am too busy during meals" -> Submit right after the meal, not during. You have a window.
- "I do not know who is responsible" -> Every classroom has one designated meal count submitter per shift. If you are that person, it is your job. If you are not, confirm who is.

**0:42 Habit commitment:**
"Starting tomorrow, your habit is: submit every meal count within 15 minutes of the meal ending. Never let a deadline go red. If you see red on the dashboard, treat it like a fire alarm."

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"What if I accidentally submit the wrong number?"** You can edit before the deadline. After the deadline, a director can make corrections with a note explaining the change.
- **"Does the system count for me automatically?"** It pre-fills based on attendance, but you must confirm and submit. The system cannot know who actually ate.
- **"What happens during a CACFP audit?"** Auditors compare your submitted counts against attendance records and menu plans. The platform keeps all three aligned, which is why accuracy matters.

### Success Indicators
- Every participant successfully submits a practice meal count
- Participants can state the cost per missed meal without looking at notes
- Each participant identifies at least one personal barrier and a solution

## M08: Delegation Workshop

**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Audience:** Directors (Tier 3), Owner (Tier 4) only

### Materials Needed
- Projector or large screen showing the task management screen
- One device per participant logged into the admin portal
- Printed "task audit" worksheet (one per person): two columns labeled "Tasks I Do" and "Tasks I Could Delegate"
- Sticky notes and pens
- Timer visible to the group

### Room Setup
Circle of chairs with a small table or clipboard for writing. This is a discussion-heavy session. Keep it informal.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:03 | Welcome and framing |
| 0:03-0:08 | Individual task audit (silent writing) |
| 0:08-0:14 | Pair share: what could you hand off? |
| 0:14-0:20 | Live demo: creating and assigning tasks in the platform |
| 0:20-0:25 | Practice: each person delegates one real task |
| 0:25-0:30 | Debrief and commitment |

### Facilitator Script

**0:00 Welcome:**
"This session is about letting go. If you are a director or owner, you probably carry tasks that someone else could handle. That is not a character flaw. It is a habit. And it costs you time you could spend on the work only you can do, like compliance, parent relationships, and strategic decisions."

"The platform has a task management system with delegation built in. Today we are going to figure out what to delegate and practice doing it."

**0:03 Individual task audit:**
"Take your worksheet. In the left column, write every task you did yesterday. Everything. Opening the building, checking email, restocking supplies, all of it. You have 5 minutes."

After 3 minutes: "Now look at your list. Circle anything that someone else on your team could do with a little training. Move those circled items to the right column."

**0:08 Pair share:**
"Turn to the person next to you. Share your 'could delegate' list. Your partner's job is to challenge you. If you kept something on the 'I do' side that could be delegated, they should push back. Two minutes each."

**0:14 Live demo:**
Pull up the task management screen. Demonstrate:
1. Creating a new task
2. Setting priority level (urgent, high, standard, low)
3. Assigning it to a specific staff member
4. Setting a due date and time block (e.g., "complete during nap time")
5. Adding clear instructions
6. Showing the assignee's view (what they see on their end)
7. Tracking completion

"Notice the nap-time optimizer. It suggests which tasks fit into the nap window based on estimated duration. That is your golden 90 minutes every day. Use it."

**0:20 Practice round:**
"Pick one task from your 'could delegate' column. Create it in the platform right now. Assign it to a real staff member. Set a real due date. Write clear instructions. Go."

Walk the room. Help with the interface. Push people to write better instructions ("restock the art supplies" is vague; "restock the art shelf in Room 2 with the supplies in the storage closet, top shelf" is clear).

**0:25 Debrief:**
- "What was hard about assigning that task?"
- "What would make you more comfortable delegating?"
- "How will you check on task completion without micromanaging?"

Listen for: control issues, trust concerns, "it is faster to do it myself" thinking. Validate those feelings, then redirect: "Faster today, but not faster over the next year. Every task you delegate once becomes a task you never do again."

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"What if they do it wrong?"** The first few times, they might. That is training, not failure. Review completed tasks and give feedback through the system.
- **"Can I see what everyone is working on?"** Yes. The task dashboard shows all assigned tasks by person, status, and due date.
- **"What about tasks that come up unexpectedly?"** Create them in the moment. It takes 30 seconds. The habit of capturing tasks in the system instead of in your head is the whole point.

### Success Indicators
- Every participant identifies at least 3 delegable tasks
- Every participant creates and assigns one real task in the platform
- At least one person expresses relief or surprise at how much they could hand off

## M11: Newsletter Best Practices

**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Audience:** Directors (Tier 3), Owner (Tier 4)

### Materials Needed
- Projector showing the newsletter creation screen
- One device per participant logged into the admin portal
- Three printed example newsletters: one excellent, one mediocre, one poor (prepare these in advance using the platform templates)
- Whiteboard and markers

### Room Setup
Tables in a small group arrangement. Participants should be able to see the projector and talk easily with each other.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:03 | Welcome and why newsletters matter |
| 0:03-0:10 | Example review: good, mediocre, and poor |
| 0:10-0:15 | Live demo: building a newsletter in the platform |
| 0:15-0:25 | Hands-on: each participant builds a newsletter |
| 0:25-0:28 | Peer review: swap and give feedback |
| 0:28-0:30 | Habit commitment and close |

### Facilitator Script

**0:00 Welcome:**
"A weekly newsletter is the single most effective way to keep families engaged and reduce the 'I did not know about that' complaints. It takes 15-20 minutes once you have a rhythm. Today we are going to build that rhythm."

**0:03 Example review:**
Hand out the three example newsletters. Give 2 minutes to read.

"Which one would you want to receive as a parent? Why?"

Guide the discussion toward these principles (write them on the whiteboard):
1. **Photos first.** Parents want to see their kids. Lead with 2-3 photos.
2. **Short sections.** Nobody reads a wall of text. Use headers and bullets.
3. **One action item.** Tell parents one thing they need to do this week.
4. **Warm tone.** Write like you are talking to a friend, not filing a report.
5. **Consistent schedule.** Every Friday by 3 PM. Families learn to expect it.

**0:10 Live demo:**
Walk through newsletter creation:
1. Select a template
2. Add a greeting section
3. Insert photos from the week (pulled from the photo gallery)
4. Add a "This Week" highlights section
5. Add an "Action Items" section (field trip permission, supply requests)
6. Add the weekly menu
7. Preview on mobile (most parents read on their phone)
8. Schedule the send for Friday at 3 PM
9. Show the read receipt dashboard after sending

**0:15 Hands-on practice:**
"Open the newsletter builder. Create a newsletter for this coming Friday. Use real photos from this week if available, or the sample photos I loaded. Include at least 3 sections. You have 10 minutes."

Walk the room. Encourage people to preview on mobile view. Give real-time feedback on length (shorter is better) and tone (warmer is better).

**0:25 Peer review:**
"Pass your device to the person on your left. Read their newsletter. Give them one thing you liked and one thing to improve."

**0:28 Close:**
"Your commitment: every Friday by 3 PM, a newsletter goes out. Put a recurring reminder on your phone right now. Fifteen minutes of effort buys you a week of parent confidence."

### Discussion Questions
- "What do parents at our center complain about most?" (Expected: not knowing what is happening, missed events, menu confusion)
- "How long do you think parents spend reading a newsletter?" (Expected: 30-60 seconds. This is why photos and bullets matter.)

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"What if nothing interesting happened this week?"** Something always happened. A child learned something new, a project was completed, a menu item was popular. If you took photos during the week, the newsletter writes itself.
- **"Can parents reply to the newsletter?"** No, newsletters are one-way. Point parents to the messaging feature for responses.
- **"How do I know if parents are reading it?"** The read receipt dashboard shows open rates. If they drop below 50%, try changing your subject line or send time.

### Success Indicators
- Every participant creates a complete newsletter with 3+ sections
- Newsletters include photos and are under 500 words
- Every participant sets a Friday 3 PM recurring reminder before leaving

## M14: CACFP Compliance PD

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Audience:** Directors (Tier 3), Owner (Tier 4)
**Format:** Full PD model with self-assessment, scenarios, reflection, and growth plan

This is a structured professional development session modeled on the Feedback-Conductance framework. It has four parts that build on each other. Do not skip or rearrange them.

### Materials Needed
- Projector showing the CACFP compliance dashboard
- One device per participant
- Printed self-assessment inventory (10 items, included below)
- Printed scenario cards (4 scenarios, included below)
- Printed reflection worksheet (included below)
- Printed growth plan template (included below)
- Pens
- Timer visible to the group

### Room Setup
Tables arranged so participants can work individually, then in pairs, then as a full group. A U-shape works well.

### Timed Agenda

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00-0:05 | Welcome, session overview, and norms |
| 0:05-0:15 | Part 1: Self-assessment inventory |
| 0:15-0:30 | Part 2: Scenario responses and discussion |
| 0:30-0:42 | Part 3: Compliance dashboard walkthrough |
| 0:42-0:50 | Part 4: Open reflection |
| 0:50-0:58 | Growth plan generation |
| 0:58-1:00 | Close and next steps |

### Part 1: Self-Assessment Inventory (0:05-0:15)

**Facilitator says:**
"Before we get into the content, I want you to take an honest look at where you are right now. This is not a test. Nobody sees your answers but you. Rate yourself honestly because the point of this exercise is to find your growth edges."

**CACFP Compliance Self-Assessment**

Rate each item from 1 (not confident at all) to 5 (completely confident):

1. I can explain what CACFP is and how reimbursement is calculated.
2. I know the meal count submission deadlines for our center.
3. I can identify the difference between Tier I and Tier II reimbursement rates.
4. I know what documentation an auditor would ask to see.
5. I can explain our center's Income Eligibility Form (IEF) process.
6. I understand the meal pattern requirements for each age group.
7. I can read the compliance dashboard and interpret the audit readiness score.
8. I know what to do when a meal count discrepancy is flagged.
9. I can generate a CACFP report from the platform for any date range.
10. I could walk a new staff member through the entire CACFP process.

**Scoring:**
- 40-50: Strong foundation. Focus on mentoring others.
- 30-39: Solid understanding with some gaps. Today will fill them.
- 20-29: Building knowledge. Pay close attention to the scenarios.
- 10-19: Starting fresh. That is completely fine. This session is designed for you.

**Facilitator says:**
"Look at your total. No need to share the number. Just notice where you scored yourself lowest. Those are the areas to focus on during the rest of this session."

### Part 2: Scenario Responses (0:15-0:30)

**Facilitator says:**
"Now we are going to work through four scenarios. For each one, I will read the situation, you will choose your response, and then we will discuss as a group. There are no trick questions, but there are better and worse answers."

**Scenario 1: The Late Count**
It is 1:45 PM on a Tuesday. You realize the lunch meal count for the Preschool Room was never submitted. The deadline was 1:30 PM. What do you do?

A) Skip it. One missed count is not a big deal.
B) Submit it now with a late notation and notify the director.
C) Wait until the end of the day and submit all counts at once.
D) Ask the teacher what happened and submit a corrected count with documentation.

*Best answer: D.* Submit the count immediately with accurate numbers. Document why it was late. Talk to the teacher about what happened so it does not recur. Option B is acceptable but does not address the root cause.

**Discussion:** "What systems could prevent this from happening? What is the cost of this one missed count?"

**Scenario 2: The Audit Visit**
A CACFP auditor arrives unannounced on a Wednesday morning. They ask to see: meal count records for the past 3 months, attendance records matching those counts, current menus, and Income Eligibility Forms. How do you respond?

A) Ask them to come back when the director is available.
B) Pull up the compliance dashboard and generate reports from the platform.
C) Go to the filing cabinet and start pulling paper files.
D) Call the owner for instructions.

*Best answer: B.* The platform stores everything the auditor needs. You can generate reports on the spot. This is exactly why digital records matter. Option D is understandable but delays the process; you should be able to handle this.

**Discussion:** "How long would it take to pull these records from paper files? How long from the platform?"

**Scenario 3: The Discrepancy**
The compliance dashboard shows a yellow flag: yesterday's lunch count (18 children) does not match the attendance record (16 children present). What do you do?

A) Ignore the flag. It is probably a system error.
B) Investigate immediately: check who was marked present, who ate, and whether the count was entered incorrectly.
C) Delete the meal count and re-enter it with 16.
D) Ask the classroom teacher what happened.

*Best answer: B, then D.* Investigate first. Maybe two children were checked in late after the attendance snapshot. Maybe the count was wrong. Never delete and re-enter without understanding the discrepancy. Document what you find.

**Discussion:** "What are the most common causes of count-attendance mismatches?"

**Scenario 4: The New Menu**
The center is changing its lunch menu to include a new entree. A staff member asks if it meets CACFP meal pattern requirements. How do you verify?

A) If it looks like a balanced meal, it is probably fine.
B) Check the CACFP meal pattern requirements in the system and verify each component.
C) Ask the cook.
D) Google it.

*Best answer: B.* The platform has meal pattern requirements built in. Check the protein, grain, vegetable/fruit, and milk components against the age group requirements. If anything is missing, adjust before serving.

**Discussion:** "What happens if an auditor finds that your menu did not meet meal pattern requirements?"

### Part 3: Compliance Dashboard Walkthrough (0:30-0:42)

**Facilitator says:**
"Now let's look at the actual data for our center. Open the compliance dashboard on your device."

Walk through:
1. Overall audit readiness score (explain what drives it up and down)
2. Meal count completion rate (percentage of meals documented on time)
3. Count-attendance alignment (are counts matching who was present?)
4. Menu compliance (are menus meeting CACFP patterns?)
5. IEF status (are all families' forms current?)
6. Generating a compliance report for a specific date range
7. Interpreting yellow and red flags
8. The "Monday habit": checking this dashboard every Monday to catch issues early

"This dashboard is your early warning system. A yellow flag today is a 5-minute fix. A red flag discovered during an audit is a $40,000 problem."

### Part 4: Open Reflection (0:42-0:50)

Hand out the reflection worksheet.

**Facilitator says:**
"Take 5 minutes to write honest answers to these three questions. This is for you, not for me."

**Reflection Prompts:**

1. What is one CACFP practice at our center that I know is not where it should be? What has prevented me from addressing it?

2. If an auditor walked in tomorrow, what would I be most worried about? What would I be most confident about?

3. What is one thing I learned today that changes how I will approach CACFP compliance this week?

After 5 minutes: "Would anyone like to share one takeaway? No pressure."

### Growth Plan Generation (0:50-0:58)

**Facilitator says:**
"Based on your self-assessment score, the scenarios, and your reflection, let's build a growth plan. This is one page. Fill it out now."

**CACFP Compliance Growth Plan**

| Field | Your Response |
|-------|--------------|
| **Biggest gap** (from self-assessment): | |
| **Evidence** (what data from the dashboard shows this gap): | |

**0:00 Welcome:**

- Renegotiating a vendor contract: varies

**0:42 Close:**

Work through this together. Help the owner be specific. "Improve enrollment" is not a priority. "Increase enrollment from 34 to 40 children by September 1 through weekly pipeline reviews and standardized tours" is a priority.

Enter priorities into the platform's strategic planning tool so they connect to the task board and calendar.

### Part 5: Open Reflection (1:10-1:20)

**Reflection Prompts:**

1. A year from now, what do I want to be different about how I run this business?

2. What am I currently spending time on that does not serve any of my strategic priorities?

3. What is the one decision I have been avoiding? What would happen if I made it this month?

Allow 7 minutes for writing. Then 3 minutes for sharing (optional, but encourage it).

### Growth Plan and Accountability (1:20-1:28)

**Strategic Leadership Growth Plan**

| Field | Your Response |
|-------|--------------|
| **Biggest leadership gap** (from self-assessment): | |
| **Evidence** (what data or pattern shows this gap): | |
| **Practice change** (one specific leadership habit): | |
| **Structural change** (one system or process to create or modify): | |
| **Strategic priorities** (list your 3-5 from Part 4): | |
| **Monthly review date** (same date each month): | |
| **Quarterly SWOT refresh date** (same date each quarter): | |
| **Annual vision check date**: | |
| **Accountability partner** (mentor, consultant, peer owner): | |

**Facilitator says:**
"The plan is only as good as the follow-through. Here is the cadence:
- Monthly: review priorities progress, check financial data, update task board
- Quarterly: refresh SWOT, adjust priorities if needed, retake self-assessment
- Annually: full retreat, new 12-month plan, vision check

Put these dates in your calendar right now. Not tomorrow. Now."

### Profile Interpretation

- **Score 40-50 (Strategic Leader):** Already operating at a high level. Focus the retreat on growth planning, legacy building, and developing the next generation of leaders in the organization.
- **Score 30-39 (Emerging Strategist):** Knows how to operate, learning how to lead. Focus on protecting strategic time and building the delegation muscle (connect back to M08).
- **Score 20-29 (Operational Leader):** Deep in the day-to-day. Focus on two things: delegating enough to create strategic time, and building one financial review habit.
- **Score 10-19 (Survival Mode):** Everything feels urgent. Focus the retreat on triage: what are the 2-3 things that matter most right now? Simplify the plan. Build stability before strategy.

### Close (1:28-1:30)

**Facilitator says:**
"You just did something most childcare owners never do: you stepped back from the building and looked at the business. You identified what is working, what is not, and where you want to go. The platform is your operating system. The strategic plan is your compass. Check both regularly. You built this business. Now lead it."

### Common Questions and Answers
- **"This feels overwhelming. Where do I start?"** Start with Priority #1. Just Priority #1. For the next 30 days, that is your focus. The other priorities do not disappear. They wait.
- **"What if my priorities conflict with each other?"** They will. That is what strategy is: choosing. If growing enrollment requires hiring, and hiring conflicts with your budget priority, you have to decide which comes first. The forecast tools from M27 can help you model the tradeoff.
- **"Should I share this plan with my staff?"** Share the priorities, yes. Staff who understand where the center is going can align their daily work. Do not share the financial details or leadership self-assessment scores. Those are yours.
- **"How often should I do this retreat?"** Once a year, with quarterly refreshes. Mark it on your calendar now.

### Success Indicators
- Owner completes a SWOT analysis with at least 5 items per quadrant
- Owner defines 3-5 strategic priorities with measurable outcomes and timelines
- Owner enters priorities into the platform's strategic planning tool
- Owner sets monthly, quarterly, and annual review dates in their calendar
- Owner identifies an accountability partner
- Self-assessment score increases by at least 5 points at the quarterly retake

## Appendix B: Transition Phrases Quick Reference

Use these phrases to move between sections smoothly. Adapt to your own voice.

**Opening a session:**
- "Thanks for being here. Let's make the most of our time together."
- "Before we start, a quick ground rule: there are no dumb questions. If you are thinking it, someone else is too."

**Moving from presentation to activity:**
- "Enough from me. Let's see how this works in practice."
- "Now it is your turn. Here is what I need you to do."

**Moving from individual work to group discussion:**
- "Let's come back together. Who wants to share first?"
- "What did you notice while working through that?"

**When the group gets off track:**
- "Great question. Let me add that to the parking lot and we will come back to it."
- "I want to honor that thought. Let's table it for now and circle back at the end."

**When someone is struggling:**
- "This is new for everyone. Take it one step at a time."
- "Let me walk you through it. There is no rush."

**Closing a session:**
- "Before you leave, I want you to name one thing you are going to do differently starting tomorrow."
- "Thank you for showing up and engaging. The platform is the tool. You are the engine."

**When energy is low:**
- "Let's take a 30-second stretch. Stand up, move around, and then we will get back to it."
- "I know this is a lot. We are [X] minutes from done. Let's finish strong."
