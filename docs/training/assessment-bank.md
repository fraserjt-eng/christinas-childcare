# Assessment Bank

Christina's Child Care Center Training Curriculum

This document contains all knowledge checks, scenario cards, self-assessment inventories, growth plan templates, and reflection prompts used across the training program. Organized by module for easy reference during facilitated sessions and self-paced learning.

---

## Part 1: Knowledge Check Questions

All questions use 4-choice format (A-D). Pass threshold: 80% per module. Unlimited retries allowed.

---

### M05: Attendance Tracking (5 Questions)

**Q1.** A parent calls at 10:15 AM to say their child will not be coming today. What should you do first in the system?

- A) Wait until end of day to mark absent
- B) Mark the child absent immediately and note the reason
- C) Ask the parent to call back later
- D) Delete the child from today's roster

**Correct: B** -- Marking absent immediately updates the real-time attendance count, which affects ratio calculations and CACFP meal count accuracy for the rest of the day.

---

**Q2.** You notice the attendance dashboard shows 14 children in the toddler room, but you count 13 in the room. What is the most likely cause?

- A) The system is broken
- B) A child checked in at the kiosk but was picked up without checking out
- C) You miscounted
- D) A child from another room was transferred

**Correct: B** -- The most common attendance discrepancy happens when a parent picks up a child without completing the kiosk check-out. This is why staff should verify attendance at every transition.

---

**Q3.** When should you check the attendance dashboard each day?

- A) Only when a parent asks
- B) At 9:30 AM after the arrival window closes
- C) At the end of the day when filing reports
- D) Only on Mondays

**Correct: B** -- The 9:30 AM check catches discrepancies while there is still time to contact families and correct records before meal counts are submitted.

---

**Q4.** Why does accurate attendance matter for CACFP reimbursement?

- A) It does not; CACFP uses enrollment numbers
- B) Meal counts must match children who were actually present and served
- C) The state only reimburses for children who arrive before 8 AM
- D) Attendance only matters for school-age children

**Correct: B** -- CACFP reimburses only for meals served to children who are present. Claiming a meal for a child marked absent is a compliance violation that can trigger an audit finding.

---

**Q5.** Two staff called in sick and the toddler room has 12 children checked in. What is the first thing you should check in the system?

- A) The newsletter schedule
- B) The ratio dashboard to see if you are still in compliance
- C) The budget report
- D) The photo gallery

**Correct: B** -- Staff absences directly affect ratios. With MN toddler ratio at 1:7, 12 toddlers require at least 2 staff. If only 1 remains, you need immediate coverage.

### M06: Meal Count Submission (10 Questions)

**Q1.** What is the CACFP reimbursement deadline for submitting breakfast counts?

- A) Before lunch
- B) By 9:30 AM
- C) End of the day
- D) End of the week

**Correct: B** -- Breakfast counts must be submitted by 9:30 AM. Late submissions risk being flagged during audits and may not be reimbursable.

---

**Q2.** Your center serves 40 children lunch daily. You miss submitting lunch counts for 3 days in one week. Using a $4.50 average reimbursement per child per meal, approximately how much revenue is at risk?

- A) $120
- B) $270
- C) $540
- D) $900

**Correct: C** -- 40 children x $4.50 x 3 days = $540. Over a year, missing just 3 days per week would cost $28,080 in lost reimbursement.

---

**Q3.** What does the "pre-fill" feature do for meal counts?

- A) Automatically submits counts without staff input
- B) Populates the meal count form with children marked present in attendance
- C) Fills in last week's numbers
- D) Sends the count directly to the state

**Correct: B** -- Pre-fill pulls from real-time attendance data so staff only need to adjust for children who were present but did not eat, or who arrived after the initial count.

---

**Q4.** A child arrives at 10:00 AM, after breakfast was served and counts were submitted. The child's parent says they did not eat breakfast at home. What should you do?

- A) Retroactively add the child to the breakfast count
- B) Offer the child a snack and note it; do not alter the submitted breakfast count
- C) Ignore the situation
- D) Submit a second breakfast count

**Correct: B** -- Once a meal count is submitted, altering it to add children who were not present during the meal is a compliance violation. The child can be offered food, but the CACFP count must reflect who was served during the meal period.

---

**Q5.** Which of the following is NOT one of the four CACFP meal types tracked in the system?

- A) Breakfast
- B) Lunch
- C) Dinner
- D) PM Snack

**Correct: C** -- The four trackable meal types for most child care centers are breakfast, AM snack, lunch, and PM snack. Dinner is only applicable for centers with extended evening hours that have a separate CACFP approval.

---

**Q6.** You notice a meal count shows 28 children served lunch, but attendance shows only 25 children present. What should you do?

- A) Submit it anyway; close enough
- B) Investigate the discrepancy before submitting
- C) Change the attendance to match the meal count
- D) Delete the meal count and skip today

**Correct: B** -- Meal counts that exceed attendance are audit red flags. The discrepancy could mean attendance was not updated, children from another room were counted twice, or the count was entered incorrectly.

---

**Q7.** How much revenue does a center with 40 children lose per year if it misses just one meal count per week at $4.50 per child?

- A) $2,340
- B) $4,680
- C) $9,360
- D) $18,720

**Correct: C** -- 40 children x $4.50 x 52 weeks = $9,360 per year from a single missed meal count each week. This is why the system shows deadlines in red when they are approaching.

---

**Q8.** A new staff member asks why meal counts matter so much. Which response is most accurate?

- A) "It's just a state requirement we have to follow."
- B) "Meal counts are how we get reimbursed through CACFP. Each missed count is money we never get back, and it adds up to thousands per year."
- C) "The director likes to see the numbers."
- D) "It helps us order the right amount of food."

**Correct: B** -- Staff who understand the financial impact of meal counts are more likely to prioritize timely submission. Framing it as "just a requirement" misses the revenue protection purpose.

---

**Q9.** The meal count deadline indicator turns yellow. What does this mean?

- A) The count was submitted successfully
- B) The deadline is approaching and the count has not been submitted yet
- C) The system is updating
- D) A child has an allergy flagged

**Correct: B** -- Yellow means the deadline is within range but not yet passed. This is the warning window to submit before the indicator turns red (deadline missed).

---

**Q10.** During a CACFP audit, the reviewer asks to see meal count records for the past 60 days. Where do you find this in the system?

- A) The photo gallery
- B) The compliance dashboard under CACFP records
- C) The parent portal
- D) The scheduling module

**Correct: B** -- The compliance dashboard stores all submitted meal counts with timestamps, child counts, and submission history. This is the primary audit-ready record.

### M08: Task Management (5 Questions)

**Q1.** It is nap time and you have 90 minutes. The task board shows 6 tasks: 2 urgent, 2 normal, 2 low priority. What should you do?

- A) Start with the easiest tasks to check them off quickly
- B) Complete the 2 urgent tasks first, then work through normal priority
- C) Skip tasks and take a break since the children are sleeping
- D) Ask someone else to do all 6

**Correct: B** -- Priority-based task completion ensures critical items (safety checks, meal prep, compliance actions) get done first. The nap window is the primary admin work block for classroom staff.

---

**Q2.** A director assigns you a task marked "urgent" to restock the first aid kit. You are about to start meal count submission with 10 minutes until deadline. What do you do?

- A) Restock the first aid kit since it is marked urgent
- B) Submit the meal count first because the deadline is imminent, then restock
- C) Do neither and wait for further instructions
- D) Ask a parent to restock

**Correct: B** -- Time-sensitive compliance tasks (meal counts with deadlines) take precedence over urgent but non-time-bound tasks. The meal count deadline is a hard cutoff; the first aid restock can happen 15 minutes later.

---

**Q3.** What is the purpose of the nap-time task optimizer?

- A) It puts children to sleep faster
- B) It suggests which tasks to complete during the nap window based on priority and estimated time
- C) It delays all tasks until naptime
- D) It assigns nap duties to staff

**Correct: B** -- The optimizer matches available time (nap window duration) with task priority and estimated completion time so staff can make the most of the uninterrupted work block.

---

**Q4.** You completed a task but forgot to mark it done in the system. Why does this matter?

- A) It does not matter; the task is done
- B) The director will still see it as incomplete, which affects task completion tracking and may trigger follow-up
- C) The system will automatically delete it
- D) Your pay will be docked

**Correct: B** -- Unmarked tasks create false signals. Directors monitoring task boards see incomplete items and may follow up unnecessarily, or worse, assume operational items were missed.

---

**Q5.** A director delegates a classroom deep-clean task to you for Friday. You have questions about what "deep clean" includes. What should you do?

- A) Ignore the task
- B) Do what you think deep clean means
- C) Check the task details in the system for the description and checklist, then ask the director if unclear
- D) Wait until Friday and ask then

**Correct: C** -- Tasks in the system include descriptions and checklists. Checking there first answers most questions. If the scope is still unclear, asking early gives the director time to clarify before the deadline.

### M14: CACFP Compliance Deep Dive (10 Questions)

**Q1.** What is the minimum audit readiness score the center should maintain on the compliance dashboard?

- A) 70%
- B) 80%
- C) 90%
- D) 100%

**Correct: C** -- The target is 90%+ audit readiness. Below 90% means there are gaps that could result in findings during a state review.

---

**Q2.** A CACFP auditor arrives unannounced. What is the first thing you should do?

- A) Ask them to come back next week
- B) Welcome them, provide access to the compliance dashboard, and notify the owner immediately
- C) Hide any incomplete records
- D) Tell them you need to check with your lawyer

**Correct: B** -- CACFP audits can be unannounced. Immediate, transparent cooperation demonstrates compliance culture. The digital compliance dashboard provides instant access to records.

---

**Q3.** How often should the compliance dashboard be reviewed?

- A) Annually before the audit
- B) Every Monday
- C) Only when there is a problem
- D) Every six months

**Correct: B** -- Weekly Monday review catches emerging issues before they compound. Waiting for the annual audit turns small gaps into systemic findings.

---

**Q4.** Which of the following would be flagged as a CACFP compliance issue?

- A) Meal counts submitted on time every day
- B) Meal counts showing more children served than were marked present
- C) A staff member uploading classroom photos
- D) A parent updating their child's emergency contact

**Correct: B** -- Meal counts exceeding attendance is a direct compliance red flag. It suggests either inaccurate attendance tracking or inflated meal counts, both of which can trigger reimbursement suspension.

---

**Q5.** The center's CACFP reimbursement was $72,000 last year. If the audit readiness score drops below 80% and triggers a review, what is the maximum amount at risk?

- A) $7,200
- B) $36,000
- C) $72,000
- D) $144,000

**Correct: C** -- A failed audit can result in suspension of the entire CACFP agreement, putting the full reimbursement amount at risk. In severe cases, the center may also need to repay previously claimed amounts.

---

**Q6.** What documents must be available for a CACFP review? Select the most complete answer.

- A) Meal counts only
- B) Meal counts, attendance records, menus, and income eligibility documentation
- C) Staff schedules and payroll
- D) Parent satisfaction surveys

**Correct: B** -- CACFP reviews examine meal counts cross-referenced with attendance, planned menus compared to actual service, and income eligibility forms for Tier 1 vs Tier 2 classification.

---

**Q7.** A compliance dashboard item shows "yellow" status for menu documentation. What does this mean?

- A) Everything is fine
- B) The item needs attention soon but is not yet a violation
- C) The item has been resolved
- D) The menu is approved

**Correct: B** -- Yellow is the warning state. It means the issue is approaching non-compliance (e.g., menu not uploaded for next week, documentation expiring soon). Addressing yellow items prevents red violations.

---

**Q8.** You discover that meal counts for the past two weeks were submitted 30 minutes late every day. What is the impact?

- A) No impact; they were still submitted the same day
- B) Late submissions may not be accepted for reimbursement and create a pattern that auditors flag
- C) The system automatically adjusts the timestamps
- D) Late counts are worth half the reimbursement

**Correct: B** -- Consistent late submissions create audit trail patterns that suggest weak internal controls. While individual late submissions may still be accepted, a pattern triggers heightened scrutiny.

---

**Q9.** What is the difference between Tier 1 and Tier 2 CACFP reimbursement?

- A) Tier 1 is for infants only
- B) Tier 1 centers receive higher reimbursement rates based on the percentage of children from low-income families in the area
- C) Tier 2 pays more than Tier 1
- D) There is no difference

**Correct: B** -- Tier 1 classification is based on the center's location (census data showing high percentage of low-income households) or the percentage of enrolled children receiving public assistance. Tier 1 rates are significantly higher.

---

**Q10.** The owner asks you to generate a compliance report for the upcoming board meeting. Where do you find this?

- A) The newsletter module
- B) The compliance dashboard export function
- C) The parent portal
- D) The scheduling module

**Correct: B** -- The compliance dashboard includes an export function that generates formatted reports showing audit readiness score, outstanding items, and trend data.

### M15: Ratio Monitoring & Compliance (10 Questions)

**Q1.** What is the Minnesota state-required staff-to-child ratio for infants (birth to 16 months)?

- A) 1:3
- B) 1:4
- C) 1:7
- D) 1:10

**Correct: B** -- Minnesota requires 1 staff for every 4 infants. Maximum group size for infants is 8.

---

**Q2.** What is the Minnesota state-required ratio for toddlers (16 to 33 months)?

- A) 1:4
- B) 1:5
- C) 1:7
- D) 1:10

**Correct: C** -- Minnesota requires 1 staff for every 7 toddlers. Maximum group size is 14.

---

**Q3.** What is the Minnesota state-required ratio for preschool children (33 months to kindergarten)?

- A) 1:7
- B) 1:8
- C) 1:10
- D) 1:12

**Correct: C** -- Minnesota requires 1 staff for every 10 preschool-age children. Maximum group size is 20.

---

**Q4.** What is the Minnesota state-required ratio for school-age children?

- A) 1:10
- B) 1:12
- C) 1:15
- D) 1:20

**Correct: C** -- Minnesota requires 1 staff for every 15 school-age children. Maximum group size is 30.

---

**Q5.** The toddler room has 13 children and 2 staff. One staff member needs to leave for 10 minutes to handle a parent concern. Are you still in ratio?

- A) Yes, 1 staff for 13 toddlers is fine
- B) No, 1 staff for 13 toddlers exceeds the 1:7 ratio; you need coverage before the staff member leaves
- C) It is fine for 10 minutes
- D) Ratios only apply during meal times

**Correct: B** -- At 1:7, 13 toddlers require 2 staff at all times. There is no grace period for brief absences. A third person must cover before either staff member leaves the room.

---

**Q6.** A parent drops off their 3-year-old at 8:05 AM. The preschool room currently has 20 children and 2 staff. What happens?

- A) Accept the child; 21 children with 2 staff is close enough
- B) The system flags a ratio violation because 21 children with 2 staff exceeds the 1:10 ratio
- C) Send the child to the toddler room
- D) Ask the parent to come back in an hour

**Correct: B** -- At 1:10, 2 staff can supervise a maximum of 20 preschool children. The 21st child creates an immediate ratio violation. A third staff member must be present before accepting the child into the room.

---

**Q7.** During afternoon departure, children leave the preschool room over 90 minutes. At what point can you release one of two staff members?

- A) When the first child leaves
- B) When 10 or fewer children remain (because 1 staff can cover 10 at 1:10 ratio)
- C) Whenever the staff member's shift ends
- D) You can never release a staff member during departure

**Correct: B** -- Once attendance drops to 10 or fewer preschool children, 1 staff member satisfies the 1:10 ratio. The system should reflect real-time attendance to support this decision.

---

**Q8.** A licensing inspector asks how you monitor ratios throughout the day. Which answer demonstrates best practice?

- A) "We count heads when we remember."
- B) "We use real-time digital attendance tracking linked to ratio monitoring, and staff check the dashboard at every transition."
- C) "We have a paper chart on the wall."
- D) "The director checks once in the morning."

**Correct: B** -- Digital real-time tracking with transition-point checks demonstrates systematic compliance, not sporadic attention.

---

**Q9.** It is 7:00 AM and only 1 staff member has arrived. 3 infants and 5 toddlers are checked in. Is the center in ratio?

- A) Yes, 1 staff for 8 children is fine
- B) No. 3 infants require 1 staff (at 1:4), and 5 toddlers require 1 staff (at 1:7). You need at least 2 staff.
- C) Ratios do not apply before 8 AM
- D) Combine the rooms and it is fine

**Correct: B** -- Ratios apply from the moment children are in care. Mixed age groups default to the ratio of the youngest children present unless rooms are separated with dedicated staff.

---

**Q10.** A ratio violation is documented during a licensing visit. What is the potential consequence?

- A) Nothing; it is just a suggestion
- B) A verbal warning with no record
- C) A licensing citation that can result in fines of $500 to $5,000 and potential conditional license status
- D) Automatic closure of the center

**Correct: C** -- Ratio violations are among the most serious licensing findings. Repeated violations can escalate from conditional license to suspension. Fines vary but a single citation commonly costs $500 to $5,000.

### M16: Incident Reporting (10 Questions)

**Q1.** A child falls on the playground and scrapes their knee. It is minor, bleeding stopped with a bandaid. Should you file an incident report?

- A) No, scrapes are normal and do not need documentation
- B) Yes, all injuries requiring any first aid must be documented
- C) Only if the parent complains
- D) Only if the child cries for more than 5 minutes

**Correct: B** -- Any injury requiring first aid, regardless of severity, must be documented. This protects the child, the staff, and the center from liability.

---

**Q2.** How quickly should an incident report be filed after the incident occurs?

- A) By end of day
- B) Within 30 minutes
- C) Within 24 hours
- D) Before the next licensing visit

**Correct: B** -- The 30-minute standard ensures details are fresh and accurate. Delayed reports lose critical details and create liability gaps.

---

**Q3.** A child bites another child. Which severity level is appropriate?

- A) Low (no documentation needed)
- B) Medium (document, notify both families same day)
- C) High (only if skin is broken)
- D) Critical (call 911)

**Correct: B** -- Biting is a medium-severity incident. Both the biting child's family and the bitten child's family must be notified. If the skin is broken, severity escalates and additional medical attention may be needed.

---

**Q4.** You file an incident report and realize 2 hours later that you got the time wrong. What should you do?

- A) Delete the report and start over
- B) Edit the report; the system tracks all changes in the audit trail
- C) Leave it; close enough
- D) File a second report with the correct time

**Correct: B** -- The system maintains a full audit trail of every edit. Correcting the time is appropriate and transparent. Deleting and recreating would look like evidence tampering to an auditor.

---

**Q5.** Which of the following incidents is licensure-reportable in Minnesota?

- A) A child refuses to eat lunch
- B) A child is transported to the emergency room from the center
- C) A parent is 5 minutes late for pickup
- D) A staff member calls in sick

**Correct: B** -- Incidents requiring emergency medical transport, allegations of maltreatment, and serious injuries are licensure-reportable. The center must notify the licensing agency within 24 hours.

---

**Q6.** A parent calls at 5:30 PM asking about a bump on their child's forehead. No incident report was filed. What happened, and what should you do?

- A) Tell the parent it probably happened at home
- B) Investigate immediately: ask staff who were in the room, review the timeline, file a late incident report with the actual time and a note explaining the delay
- C) Tell the parent you will look into it tomorrow
- D) Blame the afternoon staff

**Correct: B** -- A late report with honest documentation is far better than no report. The investigation should determine what happened, and the late filing should be noted transparently.

---

**Q7.** Two children collide heads while running in the gym. One child seems fine; the other is crying and has a visible bump. How many incident reports should you file?

- A) One, for the child with the bump
- B) Two, one for each child involved
- C) None, unless a parent asks
- D) One combined report

**Correct: B** -- Each child involved in an incident gets their own report. The "fine" child may develop symptoms later, and both families need to be informed.

---

**Q8.** What information must every incident report include?

- A) Just the child's name and what happened
- B) Child's name, date, time, location, description of what happened, injuries observed, first aid provided, staff present, parent notification status
- C) Only injuries that required a doctor visit
- D) The child's name and a photo

**Correct: B** -- Complete incident reports include all contextual details. Missing any element creates a gap that auditors, parents, or attorneys could question.

---

**Q9.** A staff member witnesses an incident but says, "I'll write it up tomorrow, I'm too busy right now." What should you do?

- A) That is fine; tomorrow is close enough
- B) Offer to help file it now, or file it yourself with available details and have them review within 30 minutes
- C) Tell them to forget about it
- D) File a complaint about the staff member

**Correct: B** -- The 30-minute window is non-negotiable. If the witnessing staff member cannot file immediately, another staff member should initiate the report with available facts.

---

**Q10.** An incident report shows that the same child has been involved in 5 biting incidents in 2 weeks. What should the director do?

- A) Nothing; some children bite
- B) Review the pattern, schedule a parent conference, develop a behavior plan, and document all steps taken
- C) Expel the child
- D) Move the child to a different classroom without telling the parents

**Correct: B** -- Patterns require systematic response: data review, family partnership, behavior planning, and documentation. Expulsion should be a last resort after documented interventions. Moving without communication damages trust.

- B) Schedule a renewal class immediately so it is complete before expiration
- C) Ignore it; 45 days is plenty of time
- D) Ask your director to renew it for you

**Correct: B** -- Scheduling immediately prevents last-minute scrambles. If a renewal class fills up or gets canceled, you need buffer time. Expired CPR means you cannot legally be counted in ratios.

- B) They cannot prepare or serve food until renewed, which may require schedule adjustments
- C) They get a written warning but continue working
- D) The center loses its license

**Correct: B** -- Expired food handling certification means the staff member cannot participate in meal prep or service. This creates an operational gap during meal times that must be covered by certified staff.

---

**Q3.** How far in advance does the system send certification expiry warnings?

- A) 7 days only
- B) 90, 60, and 30 days before expiration
- C) Only after it expires
- D) 1 year in advance

**Correct: B** -- The three-stage warning system (90/60/30 days) gives increasing urgency. The 90-day alert is for planning; the 30-day alert means action is overdue.

---

**Q4.** Minnesota requires how many annual training hours for child care staff?

- A) 4 hours
- B) 8 hours
- C) 16 hours
- D) 24 hours

**Correct: C** -- Minnesota requires 16 hours of annual in-service training for child care staff, covering topics like child development, health and safety, and behavior guidance.

---

**Q5.** The director reviews the certification dashboard and sees that 3 of 8 staff members have certifications expiring within 60 days. What is the priority action?

- A) Wait and see who renews on their own
- B) Create a renewal plan: identify which certifications, find available classes, and schedule coverage for staff attending training
- C) Hire 3 new staff members
- D) Extend the expiration dates in the system

### M18: Staff Scheduling (10 Questions)

**Q1.** Labor costs represent approximately what percentage of a child care center's total expenses?

- A) 25%
- B) 40%
- C) 56%
- D) 75%

**Correct: C** -- Labor typically represents 56% of child care center expenses. This makes scheduling optimization the single largest lever for cost control.

---

**Q2.** You are building next week's schedule for the toddler room. Enrollment is 14 toddlers with staggered arrivals (7 arrive by 7:30 AM, all 14 by 9:00 AM, departures start at 4:00 PM). What is the minimum staffing pattern?

- A) 2 staff all day (7 AM to 6 PM)
- B) 1 staff at open, 2 staff by 9 AM, back to 1 by 4:30 PM when attendance drops below 7
- C) 3 staff all day to be safe
- D) 1 staff all day since toddler ratio is 1:7

**Correct: B** -- Staggered staffing aligned to attendance patterns saves labor without violating ratios. Running 2 full shifts when 1 covers the shoulder hours wastes approximately 4 labor hours per day.

---

**Q3.** A staff member requests Friday off next week. The schedule is already published. What is the correct process?

- A) Tell them no because the schedule is published
- B) Check coverage: review ratios for Friday, find or arrange a substitute, update the schedule, and notify affected staff
- C) Grant the day off without checking coverage
- D) Delete the published schedule and start over

**Correct: B** -- Published schedules can be modified, but changes require coverage verification. The system should show whether removing that staff member creates ratio gaps.

---

**Q4.** The schedule shows 3 staff members working 42 hours each next week. The owner has a policy of avoiding overtime. What should you adjust?

- A) Nothing; 42 hours is close enough
- B) Restructure shifts so all three stay at or below 40 hours, redistributing the extra 6 total hours to part-time staff or adjusting shift start/end times
- C) Tell the staff to clock out at 40 hours even if they are still working
- D) Cancel Friday operations

**Correct: B** -- 2 hours of overtime per employee at 1.5x rate costs significantly more than redistributing those hours. The system's labor cost projection should flag this before the schedule is published.

---

**Q5.** When should next week's schedule be drafted and published?

- A) Monday morning of that week
- B) Draft by Wednesday, publish by Thursday noon
- C) The first of the month for the whole month
- D) Whenever there is time

**Correct: B** -- Wednesday drafting allows time to check ratios and resolve conflicts. Thursday noon publication gives staff 2+ days to see their schedule and raise concerns before the week starts.

---

**Q6.** The system shows a ratio compliance warning for Tuesday between 2:00 and 3:00 PM. What does this likely mean?

- A) Too many staff are scheduled
- B) A gap exists where scheduled staff do not cover the projected child count for that hour
- C) The system has a glitch
- D) A child has a doctor's appointment

**Correct: B** -- The ratio compliance view cross-references scheduled staff hours with expected attendance patterns. A warning during that window means the projected child count exceeds what scheduled staff can cover.

---

**Q7.** A center spends $340,000 annually on labor. If optimized scheduling reduces overtime by 15%, how much is saved?

- A) $5,100
- B) $10,200
- C) $25,500
- D) $51,000

**Correct: A** -- If overtime represents roughly 10% of labor ($34,000), a 15% reduction saves approximately $5,100. However, eliminating unnecessary full-time hours through staggered scheduling can yield even larger savings.

---

**Q8.** Two staff members request the same day off and only one can be approved. How should you decide?

- A) Approve whoever asked first
- B) Check who has more seniority
- C) Evaluate both requests against coverage needs, then apply the center's time-off policy (typically first-come basis, with seniority as tiebreaker)
- D) Deny both

**Correct: C** -- The decision should follow the center's documented time-off policy. The system logs request timestamps to establish first-come priority. Whatever the decision, document the reasoning.

---

**Q9.** A staff member consistently clocks in 10-15 minutes late. The schedule shows their shift starting at 7:00 AM, but they arrive at 7:10-7:15 AM. What is the scheduling impact?

- A) No impact; 15 minutes does not matter
- B) The opening staff member is alone with more children than their ratio allows for those 10-15 minutes, creating a daily compliance gap
- C) It only matters for payroll
- D) The system automatically adjusts

**Correct: B** -- Late arrivals at shift transitions are one of the most common causes of brief ratio violations. If the 7:00 AM staff member is needed to bring the room into ratio at 7:00, every late arrival is a violation.

---

**Q10.** The owner wants to reduce labor costs by $20,000 next year without reducing staff. What scheduling strategy would you recommend?

- A) Cut all staff to part-time
- B) Analyze attendance patterns to align staffing with actual child counts throughout the day, eliminating overlap hours where ratios are already met

**Correct: B** -- Recovered operational time should shift to strategic time. The most common constraint for child care owners is being consumed by daily operations with no time for business growth.

## Part 2: Scenario Cards

For facilitated group discussions. Read the card aloud, assign the role, then use the discussion prompts to guide conversation.

---

### Scenario 1: The Missing Meal Count

**Situation:** It is 1:45 PM and you just realized the lunch meal count was not submitted. The deadline was 1:30 PM. There are 36 children present and today's lunch was chicken, rice, green beans, and milk.

**Role:** You are the lead teacher in the preschool room.

**Question:** What would you do right now?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the financial impact of this one missed count? (36 children x ~$4.50 = $162)
2. What system or habit would have prevented this?
3. Who else is affected by this miss, and how do you communicate it?

**Key Learning Point:** A single missed meal count costs $100-200. Repeated misses compound to thousands annually. The habit of submitting within 15 minutes of meal end prevents deadline drift.

---

### Scenario 2: Ratio Emergency at Drop-Off

**Situation:** It is 7:15 AM. One staff member is present. Eight toddlers have been checked in, and a parent is walking in with the ninth child. The second staff member texted that they will be 30 minutes late.

**Role:** You are the opening staff member.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. At what child count did you go out of ratio? (At 8: 1:7 toddler ratio means 1 staff covers 7 max)
2. What are your immediate options? (Call director, ask parent to wait, call backup list)
3. How does the kiosk system help you see this coming?

**Key Learning Point:** Ratios apply from the moment children are in your care. Having a backup coverage plan and monitoring the attendance dashboard at opening prevents ratio violations during arrival rushes.

---

### Scenario 3: The Unannounced Auditor

**Situation:** A state CACFP auditor arrives at 10:30 AM and asks to see meal count records for the past 90 days, today's attendance, the posted menu, and income eligibility documentation.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Can you pull all four items within 5 minutes using the system?
2. What is the difference between scrambling and being prepared?
3. What is at risk financially if any of these records are incomplete?

**Key Learning Point:** Continuous audit readiness (90%+ on the compliance dashboard) eliminates panic. The system stores everything the auditor needs. The director's job is to know where to find it.

---

### Scenario 4: The Biting Pattern

**Situation:** A 2-year-old has bitten three different children in the past week. Two parents have complained. The biting child's parents say "all toddlers bite" and seem unconcerned.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What does the incident report data show about when and where the biting occurs?
2. How do you balance the biting child's needs with the safety of other children?
3. What documentation protects the center if the situation escalates?

**Key Learning Point:** Pattern data from incident reports informs the behavior plan. Documentation of interventions, parent communication, and professional consultation protects the center legally and demonstrates good faith effort.

---

### Scenario 5: The Enrollment Leak

**Situation:** The pipeline shows 12 inquiries in the past month but only 2 tours scheduled and 0 enrollments. The infant room has 2 open spots worth $1,400/month each.

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Where is the pipeline breaking down? (Inquiry to tour conversion: 17%. Tour to enrollment: 0%.)
2. What follow-up system should be in place, and is it being used?
3. What is the monthly cost of those 2 empty infant spots? ($2,800/month, $33,600/year)

**Key Learning Point:** Enrollment pipeline is revenue infrastructure. Empty spots are not "we haven't found the right family yet"; they are $33,600/year in lost revenue. Systematic follow-up converts inquiries to tours to enrollments.

---

### Scenario 6: The Budget Surprise

**Situation:** The accountant calls in October to say the center has spent $22,000 more than budgeted year-to-date. The largest overages are in labor ($14,000 over) and supplies ($8,000 over).

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. If the budget dashboard had been reviewed monthly, when would this have been caught?
2. What specific data would you pull to understand the labor overage? (Overtime reports, scheduling versus actual hours, staffing ratios versus enrollment)
3. What can you do in the remaining 3 months to close the gap?

**Key Learning Point:** Budget surprises only happen when budgets are not reviewed. Monthly review with the dashboard catches variances at $2,000-3,000, not $22,000. The earlier you see a drift, the more options you have.

---

### Scenario 7: The Certification Crunch

**Situation:** You review the certification dashboard and discover that 4 of your 8 staff members have CPR certifications expiring within the next 45 days. The next available CPR class in the area has 6 spots and is 3 weeks away.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. If all 4 expire before the class, how does this affect daily operations?
2. What scheduling changes do you need to make to send staff to training?
3. What system would have prevented 4 simultaneous expirations?

**Key Learning Point:** Staggering certification renewal dates prevents batch expiration crises. The 90-day alert exists to give time for class scheduling. Four simultaneous expirations suggest the alerts were ignored.

---

### Scenario 8: The Late Pickup

**Situation:** It is 6:10 PM. The center closes at 6:00 PM. One child remains and the parent has not arrived or called. Two staff members are still present (required by licensing). Neither can leave until the child is picked up.

**Role:** You are the closing staff member.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the overtime cost of 2 staff members staying 30 extra minutes? (~$16/hr x 1.5 OT x 2 staff x 0.5 hr = $24)
2. What is the parent communication protocol for late pickup?
3. At what point do you contact emergency contacts versus the parent?

**Key Learning Point:** Late pickups are an operational cost (overtime), a staffing burden, and a parent accountability issue. A documented late pickup policy with fees ($1/minute is common) reduces chronic late pickups.

---

### Scenario 9: The New Staff First Day

**Situation:** A new staff member starts today. She arrives at 7:00 AM for her first shift. Her background check is clear, but when you pull up her onboarding checklist, her CPR certification and mandatory reporter training are not yet completed.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Can she be alone in a room with children? (No. She cannot be counted in ratios.)
2. How does this affect today's staffing plan?
3. What should have happened before today to prevent this situation?

**Key Learning Point:** Onboarding compliance items must be completed before the first shift, not during it. A new hire who arrives without required certifications is a supernumerary, not a ratio-counted staff member, until cleared.

---

### Scenario 10: The Parent Complaint

**Situation:** A parent sends a message at 4:30 PM: "My child came home with a bruise on her arm and nobody told me about it. This is unacceptable." There is no incident report in the system for today.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the immediate communication step?
2. What investigation should happen internally?
3. If the bruise occurred at the center and no report was filed, what is the compliance and liability exposure?

**Key Learning Point:** Unreported incidents create the highest liability risk. A documented incident with parent notification demonstrates transparency. An undocumented incident discovered by the parent suggests negligence or concealment.

---

### Scenario 11: The Revenue Forecast Warning

**Situation:** The revenue forecast shows that if 2 currently enrolled families leave (they have been touring other centers), monthly revenue drops below the breakeven point of $36,750.

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What retention actions could prevent these families from leaving?
2. What pipeline activity should accelerate to backfill if they do leave?
3. What expense reductions would bridge the gap during a 2-month vacancy?

**Key Learning Point:** Forecasting is not about predicting the future perfectly. It is about seeing risk early enough to act. Two at-risk families is a signal to activate both retention and recruitment.

---

### Scenario 12: The Schedule Conflict

**Situation:** Two staff members in the toddler room both request the same Friday off for personal reasons. Approving both would leave the room with 1 staff member for 14 toddlers, a clear ratio violation.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What does your time-off policy say? (First-come? Seniority? Neither documented?)
2. What are the options? (Approve one, find coverage for the other; deny both; split the day)
3. How do you communicate the decision fairly?

**Key Learning Point:** Time-off conflicts are inevitable. A documented, transparent policy applied consistently prevents accusations of favoritism. The system logs request timestamps to establish first-come priority.

---

### Scenario 13: The CACFP Deadline Cascade

**Situation:** It is the last day of the month. Your monthly CACFP claim is due. You discover that meal counts for 4 days this month were never submitted.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Can the 4 missing days be recovered? (Depends on state policy; some allow late submissions with documentation, others do not.)
2. What is the financial impact of 4 missing days? (40 children x $4.50 x 4 meals x 4 days = $2,880 at risk)
3. What daily habit would prevent this from happening again?

**Key Learning Point:** Monthly CACFP claims are only as good as daily meal count submissions. End-of-month scrambles to reconstruct missing data create audit risk and may not recover the revenue.

---

### Scenario 14: The Strategic Drift

**Situation:** The owner set 3 strategic priorities in January: improve staff retention, increase infant enrollment, and launch a parent newsletter. It is now June. The newsletter launched, but retention is worse and infant enrollment has not changed.

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Why did the newsletter succeed but the other two priorities stall? (Newsletter is a task; retention and enrollment are systems that require sustained effort.)
2. What specific actions with deadlines would move retention and enrollment forward?
3. Is the quarterly SWOT refresh happening? What would it reveal?

**Key Learning Point:** Strategic priorities that lack specific weekly actions and accountability structures become wishes. The meeting efficiency tool converts priorities into tracked action items with owners and deadlines.

---

### Scenario 15: The Mixed-Age Morning

**Situation:** At 6:30 AM, the center opens with 1 staff member. By 7:00 AM, 2 infants, 4 toddlers, and 3 preschoolers have arrived. All are in one room.

**Role:** You are the opening staff member.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Which ratio applies in a mixed-age group? (The youngest children's ratio: infant 1:4)
2. At what point did the room go out of ratio? (When the 5th child arrived, since 1 staff at 1:4 covers maximum 4)
3. What staffing structure prevents this?

**Key Learning Point:** Mixed-age groups default to the most restrictive ratio. Morning arrival plans must account for staggered staff arrivals matching projected child arrivals by age group.

---

### Scenario 16: The Overtime Spiral

**Situation:** Payroll review shows that 3 staff members each worked 45+ hours for the past 3 weeks. The schedule shows them at 40 hours, but they are staying late for parent pickups, cleaning, and "just finishing up."

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the weekly overtime cost? (3 staff x 5 OT hrs x $24/hr = $360/week, $18,720/year)
2. Is the overtime caused by understaffing, poor scheduling, or culture ("everyone stays late")?
3. What structural change (staggered shifts, closing procedures, additional part-time staff) would eliminate the overtime?

**Key Learning Point:** Unauthorized overtime is often invisible until payroll review. The system flags hours exceeding 40, but the root cause must be addressed structurally, not just by telling people to leave on time.

---

### Scenario 17: The Tour No-Show

**Situation:** A family was scheduled for a tour at 10:00 AM. You prepared the enrollment packet, briefed the classroom, and cleared your schedule. It is now 10:30 AM and they have not arrived or responded to your confirmation message.

**Role:** You are the director.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the appropriate follow-up timeline? (Same day text/email, then 48-hour follow-up)
2. How many tour no-shows happen per month, and what is the conversion impact?
3. What would reduce no-shows? (24-hour confirmation, day-of reminder, easy reschedule link)

**Key Learning Point:** Tour no-shows are a pipeline metric. If no-show rates exceed 20%, the confirmation process needs strengthening. Each no-show delays potential enrollment by days or weeks.

---

### Scenario 18: The Allergy Incident

**Situation:** During afternoon snack, a new staff member serves crackers with peanut butter to all children. A 3-year-old with a documented peanut allergy eats one before another staff member notices.

**Role:** You are the classroom lead teacher.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. What is the immediate medical response? (Check the child's allergy action plan, administer EpiPen if indicated, call 911 if symptoms appear)
2. What is the reporting chain? (Incident report immediately, call parent, notify director, document everything)
3. What system failure allowed this? (New staff not briefed on allergies, allergy list not posted, no snack preparation check)

**Key Learning Point:** Allergy management is a life-safety system, not a preference. The child profile in the system flags allergies. The onboarding checklist includes "review classroom allergy list." Both must be verified, not assumed.

---

### Scenario 19: The Dual-Site Decision

**Situation:** Brooklyn Park has a waitlist of 5 families for the toddler room. Crystal has 3 open toddler spots but fewer inquiries. A Brooklyn Park family asks if you have any openings.

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Should you direct families from one site to another? What are the benefits and risks?
2. What data from the cross-site dashboard informs this decision? (Distance for families, quality metrics at each site, staff capacity)
3. How do you market the Crystal openings to Brooklyn Park overflow without making Crystal feel like the "second choice"?

**Key Learning Point:** Multi-site operations create referral opportunities. The cross-site dashboard shows where capacity exists. Directing overflow between sites maximizes total enrollment without competing with yourself.

### Scenario 20: The Year-End Strategic Review

**Situation:** It is December. The owner reviews the year: enrollment is up 8%, CACFP compliance is at 94%, but staff turnover was 35% and the budget ended 7% over. The strategic plan had 5 priorities; 2 were completed.

**Role:** You are the owner.

**Question:** What would you do for next year's plan?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. Which metrics improved and why? Which declined and why?
2. What should next year's top 3 priorities be, based on this data?
3. How do you set priorities that are ambitious enough to matter but realistic enough to complete?

**Key Learning Point:** Annual strategic review connects data to decisions. Celebrating the 8% enrollment growth and 94% compliance reinforces what is working. Addressing 35% turnover and budget overages focuses next year's energy where it is needed most.
