## Scene 2: Financial Overview — Scenario Cards (50 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#financials`. Hold on Scenario A card, then B, then C. Linger on C with the recommended badge.
**VOICE:** Three scenarios. Scenario A is what happens if nothing changes. Both centers run on paper, spreadsheets, and texts. The owner manages by physically driving between buildings. The cost is not what you spend. It is what you lose: sixty-two to one hundred twelve thousand dollars a year in revenue never collected, time never recovered, and families never retained. Over three years that compounds to as much as three hundred thirty-six thousand. Scenario B deploys the platform at Brooklyn Park only. The new center starts with digital operations, but Crystal stays manual. Net year one savings: nineteen to forty-four thousand. The catch: you are running two different systems at two locations during the most critical period of your expansion. Scenario C deploys to both. One dashboard. One training system. Full visibility. The marginal cost of adding Crystal is eight hundred fifty-nine dollars in hardware and staff training time. Zero additional software cost. Net year one: thirty-six to seventy-five thousand.

## Scene 6: Brooklyn Park (35 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#bp`. Show the two-panel intro (Brooklyn Park vs. Crystal positioning), then the yellow "one-time opportunity" banner, then the 6-step setup.
**VOICE:** Brooklyn Park is a different kind of deployment than Crystal. A new center has no legacy system to replace. No staff habits to unlearn. No families expecting a different process. If the platform is in place before the first child walks in, there is no transition. Only adoption. New staff complete thirty training modules before opening day. The iPad is at the front desk. CACFP tracking starts from the first meal. The parent portal is live when the first family enrolls. That version of deployment, pure adoption with no transition, only exists at the very beginning. The six steps on screen take about three hours of technical setup plus two weeks of self-paced staff training.

**SCREEN:** Scroll through `#aug`, `#sept`, `#oct`, `#nov`, pausing 8-10 seconds on each card header and badge.
**VOICE:** August: back to school season. Enrollment peaks, new families arriving, staff adjusting to fall schedules. ## Scene 13: What It Replaces (45 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#replaces`. Slow scroll through all 14 rows of the replacement table.
**VOICE:** This is the section I want you to spend the most time with on your own. Every row is a manual process you do today on the left, and the platform feature that replaces it on the right. Paper sign-in becomes an iPad kiosk. Hand-written meal counts become automated compliance scoring. Texting parents one by one becomes a portal with announcements and read receipts. The whiteboard schedule becomes an optimizer that flags overtime before it happens. Supply requests by text become a tracked inventory system with threshold alerts. Revenue estimates on a napkin become a forecasting tool with scenario modeling. The time column on the right adds up to eight to twelve hours per week. That is your time. The platform gives it back to you.

## Scene 14: Family Impact (25 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#families`. Show the four impact cards.
**VOICE:** For families: daily photos so parents feel connected to their child's day. Self-service access so they stop calling the front desk for basic information. Digital progress reports that persist across the child's entire enrollment. Real-time attendance so authorized contacts can see pickup status. Research shows families receiving daily visual updates are significantly less likely to leave. That retention is worth sixteen to thirty thousand dollars a year.
**TIMING:** 25s

## Scene 15: Staff Impact (25 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#staff`. Show the four impact cards.
**VOICE:** For staff: structured onboarding through thirty interactive modules instead of shadowing someone for two weeks and hoping things stick. A knowledge base so institutional knowledge does not walk out the door when someone leaves. Certification tracking with automatic alerts at ninety, sixty, and thirty days. Self-service scheduling, pay stubs, and time-off requests so staff are not constantly asking the director for things they should be able to see themselves.
**TIMING:** 25s

## Scene 16: Compliance (20 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#compliance`. Show the licensing table.
**VOICE:** Minnesota DCYF licensing violations carry fines of one hundred to one thousand dollars each, with correction orders and potential suspension for serious findings. The platform gives you real-time ratio monitoring, automated CACFP compliance scoring, timestamped attendance records, incident reporting with audit trails, and certification expiry alerts. If an inspector visits, your documentation is already done.
**TIMING:** 20s

## Scene 17: Deployment Plan (25 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#deploy`. Show the two deployment checklists side by side (Brooklyn Park and Crystal).
**VOICE:** Brooklyn Park setup: create the database, set five environment variables, configure owner login, enter employee data, install the iPad, and have staff complete training. Total: about three hours of technical work plus two weeks of self-paced training. Adding Crystal is the same process. Three more hours. Zero additional software cost. The one thousand a year covers both.
**TIMING:** 25s

## Scene 18: Risk Analysis (30 seconds)

**SCREEN:** Scroll to `#risk`. Show the "not adopting" risk cards (red HIGH badges), then the "adopting" risk cards (green LOW badges).
**VOICE:** Six risks of not adopting, all rated high or medium: CACFP revenue loss, enrollment leakage, staff turnover, owner burnout, licensing fines, parent attrition. Four risks of adopting, all rated medium or low: staff resistance, mitigated by the built-in training. Internet outage, mitigated by offline local storage. Developer dependency, mitigated by standard technology. Data loss, mitigated by dual-write architecture and automated backups. The risk profile is not close.
